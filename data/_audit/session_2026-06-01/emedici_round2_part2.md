## New patterns / refinements

### R1. The "embryology one-step" question (img 002 to 003, Q4 paeds)

A neonate born at term via normal vaginal delivery, Apgar scores were eight and nine at one and five minutes respectively. On routine newborn examination, femoral pulses are difficult to palpate compared to brachial pulses. The four limb blood pressures show a gradient of 25 mmHg between the upper limbs and lower limbs. Echocardiography confirms a discrete coarctation of the aorta distal to the left subclavian artery.

Lead-in: "Which one of the following embryological structures is implicated in the development of this boy's condition?"

Options: truncus arteriosus, ductus venosus, ductus arteriosus (CORRECT), foramen ovale, ligamentum arteriosum.

Part-1 craft-move significance:

- This is the only round-2 question that asks a pure embryology / developmental-anatomy fact in the option row. The clinical scenario in the stem is fully diagnostic of coarctation (radio-femoral delay, four-limb BP gradient quoted to 25 mmHg, echo confirming "distal to the left subclavian"), and the lead-in then pivots from "diagnose" to "name the implicated embryological precursor". This is a clean variant of the lead-in pivot pattern but applied to anatomy / development rather than to drug mechanism or test choice.
- Trick is that the ligamentum arteriosum is the post-natal REMNANT of the ductus arteriosus and is therefore a near-miss option. The diagnosis is correct but candidates who pattern-match "coarctation - aorta - ligamentum" pick the wrong option.
- Stem includes the BP gradient AS a number ("25 mmHg") rather than as an adjective ("significant") - confirms A8.
- Discriminator: candidates must know that abnormal narrowing of the aorta in the region of the ductus arteriosus is the failure-of-regression-of-ductus-tissue mechanism, not the failure-of-the-remnant-ligament.

Adoption: write at least one paeds embryology question per cluster (TOF, VSD, ASD, transposition, hypoplastic left heart, persistent truncus, coarctation, PDA). Lead-in template: "Which one of the following embryological structures is most directly implicated in this condition?"

### R2. The "definitive management with side-specification" lead-in (img 036 to 037, Q27 ectopic)

A 32-year-old woman presents to the Emergency Department with a three-day history of sudden onset severe right-sided lower abdominal pain rated 8/10. She reports a positive home pregnancy test one week ago and estimates she is six weeks pregnant. She has a history of left tubal ectopic pregnancy two years ago requiring a left salpingectomy. She has no other significant medical history... transvaginal ultrasound shows an empty uterus and a right adnexal mass with a gestational sac and a small amount of free fluid in the pouch of Douglas. Laparoscopy confirms an unruptured ectopic pregnancy in the right fallopian tube.

Lead-in: "Which one of the following is the most appropriate surgical management?"

Options: right salpingostomy, right salpingectomy (CORRECT), methotrexate, right tubal ligation, right partial salpingectomy.

New craft moves:

- The stem includes the side specification (right adnexal, right fallopian tube) and a contralateral history (prior left salpingectomy two years ago) - the side specification is the discriminator because the only remaining tube is the right, so SALPINGOTOMY (preserve tube) would normally be preferred for fertility preservation, BUT the patient already has only one tube and the rationale notes "salpingostomy is preferred over salpingectomy when the patient still has the contralateral tube". With a prior contralateral salpingectomy, the recurrence-of-ectopic risk in the remaining tube outweighs fertility preservation.
- This is a NEW trickiness lever beyond T1 to T13: "the historical-bilaterality flip" - prior treatment to the contralateral organ inverts the standard fertility-preservation answer. Add as T14.
- Options use parallel side+procedure grammar ("right salpingostomy" vs "right salpingectomy" vs "right tubal ligation" vs "right partial salpingectomy"). Methotrexate is unsided (it is systemic) which is a deliberate parallel-grammar break that maps to a different decision axis (medical vs surgical).

Adoption: when the management decision depends on the patient's OWN prior surgical history, the prior history must be in the stem as a concrete event with a date, not as "previous surgery". The decision-axis word ("definitive surgical") in the lead-in scopes away from medical management without giving the answer away.

### R3. Chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM) vs otitis externa - the part-1 conflation (img 070 to 072, Q10 paeds)

Part 1 listed this as "otitis externa". The actual stem is an eight-year-old with several-week history of right ear discharge AND right ear pain (not the canal pruritis / hot tub history that defines otitis externa), and Weber's test lateralises to the right. Otoscopy shows a perforated tympanic membrane with mucopurulent discharge in the middle ear.

This is CHRONIC SUPPURATIVE OTITIS MEDIA (CSOM), not otitis externa. The discriminator is the perforated TM with middle ear discharge AND conductive hearing loss.

Correct answer: ciprofloxacin ear drops. Reasoning: when the tympanic membrane is perforated, topical aminoglycosides (dexamethasone + framycetin + gramicidin) are AVOIDED due to risk of ototoxicity from middle-ear exposure. Ciprofloxacin alone is the first-line topical agent because it is not ototoxic.

Part-1 missed this. The mined fact is the CSOM-vs-externa discrimination, not the antibiotic class generically. Update mined-facts section: see below.

### R4. The "deliberate parallel-grammar break" in option rows

In Q27 ectopic, four options share "right [procedure]" grammar and methotrexate breaks parallel. Across round-2 questions, this break consistently signals "this option belongs to a different management axis" (medical vs surgical, screening vs diagnostic, observation vs intervention). Where part 1's C-rules said dose-format parity within an option set, R4 refines: parallel grammar is the default, and a deliberate break is a legitimate signal that the option lives on a different axis. The candidate must still parse why the axis is different - it is not a strawman.

Audit rule: when an option breaks parallel grammar with the others, its rationale must say one sentence on why it lives on a different decision axis. If the break is accidental (cosmetic / draft artifact), fix it.

### R5. Lab-value laddering inside one stem (img 008 to 009, Q9 thalassaemia trait)

Part 1 listed this question's topic but did not capture the lab block style. The stem includes the following inline laboratory results, indented under "Blood tests reveal":

- Haemoglobin 100 g/L (115 to 160)
- Red blood cells 6.06 x 10^12/L (3.8 to 5.8)
- Mean cell volume 66 fL (80 to 100)
- Mean cell haemoglobin 17% (32 to 36) [reported as a percentage where it would normally be picograms - emedici quirk, see G19 below]
- White cell count, within normal limits
- Platelet count, within normal limits

The blood film shows microcytosis, hypochromia and occasional target cells.

Craft moves:

- Each abnormal lab has the patient value AND the reference range in parentheses; normal results are explicitly stated as "within normal limits" rather than enumerated with values. This is a cleaner alternative to our pattern of always quoting the number with reference range; for cases where the candidate only needs to know that the line is normal, the words "within normal limits" save vertical space.
- The high red-blood-cell count (6.06 x 10^12/L, above the reference range) is a deliberate disconfirming finding against iron-deficiency anaemia (where RBC is typically low or low-normal) and a confirming finding for thalassaemia trait (where the marrow over-produces small cells, so RBC count is preserved or elevated despite low haemoglobin). The candidate who notices this number wins the question.
- Blood film descriptor sits AS A SEPARATE SENTENCE after the lab block, not as part of the block. This is correct - microscopy is a different modality.

Adoption: add "within normal limits" as a permitted shorthand when a result is normal and the absence of derangement is itself informative. Reserve the full numeric form for any line where the value is the discriminator or is being deliberately disconfirming.

### R6. The "history is the disconfirming feature" pattern at sentence-2 of the stem

In several round-2 stems (Q9 thalassaemia trait, Q27 ectopic, Q41 aspirin timing) the very SECOND sentence of the stem carries the disconfirming-or-pivot finding. Part 1's A1 says "one late anchor"; R6 refines: the anchor can be late OR can be the second sentence when the diagnosis is otherwise too obvious. Q9 sentence 2: "She has no significant medical history and takes no regular medications. She eats a balanced diet, has regular menstrual periods (the standard menstrual closure), and takes no other medications." Then sentence 3 introduces "Her mother has a known thalassaemia trait" which is the discriminating family history. This is the SAME mechanism as the late-stem anchor, applied at the top instead of the bottom. Both are valid. Random distribution across positions is what we want, not always-late placement.

### R7. The "lateralising finding" inside an otherwise routine paeds exam

R3 (CSOM) used "Weber's test lateralises to the right" as the single piece of bedside-test data that points the candidate to conductive hearing loss on the affected side. The principle: ONE simple bedside-test result, named by the test rather than by the abstract finding, is enough to anchor the diagnosis without a full neurological examination. Adopt: "Weber lateralises to the [side]", "Romberg positive", "Trendelenburg negative", "Cover test reveals an outward shift of the left eye" - each names the test, not the finding. Avoid the prose form "there is unilateral hearing loss on the right" which collapses the test step.

## New mined facts

Grouped by module. AU spellings; named sources on first mention.

### Paediatrics

- Coarctation of the aorta - embryological basis: arises from abnormal regression or persistence of tissue at the site of the ductus arteriosus; preferential narrowing in the region of the ductus arteriosus distal to the left subclavian artery. The ductus arteriosus (not the ligamentum arteriosum, its post-natal remnant) is the embryological structure whose abnormal involution underlies juxtaductal coarctation. Pre-ductal coarctation in neonates can present with cardiogenic shock when the ductus closes; post-ductal coarctation in older children presents with upper-limb hypertension, radio-femoral delay, and a four-limb BP gradient. Diagnostic four-limb BP gradient of greater than 20 mmHg between upper and lower limbs is supportive; echocardiography is the first-line diagnostic test in neonates.
- Chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM): distinct from acute otitis media (less than 3 weeks) and otitis externa (canal pathology). Defined by chronic (greater than 2 to 6 weeks) ear discharge through a perforated tympanic membrane. First-line topical treatment is ciprofloxacin ear drops because the perforation exposes the middle ear to topical agents and aminoglycoside-containing drops (framycetin, gentamicin, neomycin) carry an ototoxicity risk when applied to a perforated TM. Quinolone ear drops (ciprofloxacin, ofloxacin) are non-ototoxic and are the preferred first-line agent. Per Therapeutic Guidelines (eTG) and Royal Children's Hospital Melbourne Clinical Practice Guidelines (RCH CPG). Oral antibiotics (amoxicillin-clavulanate, flucloxacillin) are not first-line and are reserved for complications such as mastoiditis or systemic spread. Audiology assessment is part of follow-up; surgical referral (myringoplasty / tympanoplasty) is for persistent perforation or recurrent discharge.
- Otitis externa (uncomplicated, intact TM): aminoglycoside-containing combination drops (dexamethasone + framycetin + gramicidin) ARE first-line per eTG and RCH CPG, because the intact TM prevents middle-ear exposure to the aminoglycoside. The "intact vs perforated TM" check is the binary that selects between aminoglycoside-containing drops and quinolone-only drops.
- Thalassaemia trait laboratory pattern: low haemoglobin with PRESERVED or ELEVATED red cell count (RBC 5 to 7 x 10^12/L despite Hb in the 90 to 110 g/L range); markedly low MCV (60 to 75 fL); blood film with microcytosis, hypochromia, target cells, basophilic stippling. Iron studies typically normal (contrast iron deficiency where RBC is low). Definitive test is haemoglobin electrophoresis showing elevated HbA2 (greater than 3.5%) for beta-thalassaemia trait; alpha-thalassaemia trait usually requires genetic studies. Family history is a strong clue. Mentzer index (MCV divided by RBC) less than 13 suggests thalassaemia trait; greater than 13 suggests iron deficiency.
- Sideroblastic anaemia: dimorphic blood picture with hypochromic and normochromic populations; ringed sideroblasts on iron-stained bone marrow; serum iron and ferritin are typically elevated. Causes include hereditary X-linked, myelodysplasia, lead poisoning, and drug-induced (isoniazid, chloramphenicol).
- Lead poisoning anaemia: microcytic with basophilic stippling on blood film; history may include abdominal pain, neurological symptoms, occupational or environmental exposure (old paint, batteries, contaminated water).

### Obstetrics and Gynaecology

- Ectopic pregnancy with a prior contralateral salpingectomy: standard teaching favours salpingostomy (preserves tube, retains future fertility potential through the remaining tube) over salpingectomy when the other tube is intact. When the patient has a prior contralateral salpingectomy (i.e. the affected tube is the only remaining tube), recurrence of ectopic in the SAME tube becomes the dominant risk; salpingectomy is the preferred definitive management. The rate of recurrent ectopic in a previously affected tube after salpingostomy is approximately 15% per RANZCOG (Royal Australian and New Zealand College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists) guidance.
- Salpingostomy is preferred over salpingectomy when the contralateral tube is present and apparently normal, particularly when the woman wishes to preserve fertility. Single-tube salvage probability of future natural conception is approximately 70% after salpingostomy in this scenario.
- Methotrexate medical management of ectopic: criteria include haemodynamic stability, no significant abdominal pain, beta-hCG less than approximately 5000 IU/L, no fetal cardiac activity, ectopic mass less than 3.5 cm, and patient compliance with follow-up. Not appropriate when there is active intra-abdominal bleeding or when surgical confirmation by laparoscopy has already been undertaken with the patient anaesthetised.
- Maternal serum AFP screening at 15 to 18 weeks: addresses the gap that non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT) does NOT cover - open neural tube defects (anencephaly, open spina bifida) and ventral wall defects (omphalocoele, gastroschisis). When a woman opts for NIPT instead of combined first-trimester screening, MSAFP at 15 to 20 weeks remains the appropriate screen to add for structural anomalies that NIPT does not detect. Mid-trimester anatomy ultrasound at 18 to 20 weeks is the more sensitive standalone screen for structural anomalies but the discrete blood-test step (MSAFP) is the answer for "what should be offered alongside NIPT" before the anatomy scan window.
- Coarctation of the aorta in pregnancy: associated with increased maternal cardiovascular morbidity (hypertension, aortic dissection); echocardiography in pre-pregnancy counselling. Severe coarctation requires repair before pregnancy. Aortic dissection risk is highest in second and third trimester and immediately postpartum.

### Medicine

- Thalassaemia trait differential considerations (also applies to general medicine cluster): the high RBC count is a sentinel finding; iron deficiency is the most common cause of microcytic anaemia but does NOT typically elevate RBC. Order: full blood count + film + iron studies + haemoglobin electrophoresis. Family history and ethnic background (Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, South-East Asian, sub-Saharan African) are important for haemoglobin variants beyond classic beta-thalassaemia.

## What emedici does WRONG (additions to part 1's list)

### G19. Inconsistent unit-format on lab results within one stem

Q9 (thalassaemia trait) reports mean cell haemoglobin as "17% (32 to 36)" where the unit should be picograms (pg) for mean cell haemoglobin or percent for mean cell haemoglobin concentration (MCHC). The reference range given (32 to 36) is the MCHC range in g/dL or as percent; 17 is below normal for MCHC but the lab value "17%" is ambiguous between MCH and MCHC. emedici conflates these. For our bank: choose ONE of MCH (pg) or MCHC (g/L or %) per lab block, never mix. Audit rule: any haematology lab block must use AU/Australasian RCPA reference ranges (Royal College of Pathologists of Australasia) and explicit units on every line.

### G20. Embryology question dressed as a clinical question

Q4 paeds presents a long clinical scenario for coarctation then asks an embryology question. The lead-in pivot is a legitimate craft move (R1) but it is BORDERLINE on D4 / "named source" because the embryology fact does not have a clinical-society guideline behind it - it is anatomy/development textbook knowledge. emedici cites "References 3" with no named source. For our bank: when the answer is a textbook anatomical / embryological fact, cite a standard textbook by name (e.g. Moore's The Developing Human, Sadler's Langman's Medical Embryology) at first mention in the rationale. Anonymous "References 3" is not acceptable.

### G21. Part-1's "otitis externa" mis-labelling propagated from emedici's own taxonomy

emedici lists the CSOM question under an "Otitis externa / outer ear" cluster in their question-bank index (visible on the right-pane breadcrumb when expanded). This taxonomy collapses CSOM (middle ear disease) and otitis externa (canal disease) under the same heading. Our cluster structure must keep them distinct: CSOM under "Middle ear" with chronic and acute OM; otitis externa under "External ear / canal".

### G22. The "ligamentum arteriosum" / "ductus arteriosus" near-pair as a distractor without an explanatory rationale

In Q4 paeds, the rationale paragraph for ligamentum arteriosum says only "Ligamentum arteriosum is incorrect" without explaining the post-natal remnant relationship. This is a pseudo-D3 (per G16). The defensible answer-rationale would say: "The ligamentum arteriosum is the post-natal fibrous remnant of the ductus arteriosus; abnormal development of the ligamentum is not implicated in coarctation, because coarctation arises from the EMBRYOLOGICAL precursor (ductus arteriosus) rather than its post-natal remnant." Audit rule: every near-miss option must explain WHY it is near-miss, not just assert wrongness.

### G23. Single-modality "References N" without expansion

Part 1 noted this at G13. Round-2 reconfirms: most rationales close with "References 3" or "References 1" as a deferred citation count. Where the actual reference is one inline link plus two unnamed sources, the count is misleading. Our pattern: every rationale ends with a named-source line ("Source: Royal Australian and New Zealand College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists Guideline, 2021"), not a count.

### G24. "Most clinically significant diagnosis" lead-in phrasing (img 038 to 039, Q29 placenta accreta)

The lead-in reads "Which one of the following is the most clinically significant diagnosis in this case?" The phrase "most clinically significant" is a soft synonym for "most worrying" and is borderline-telegraphing (it leans the candidate toward the most morbid option). Cleaner: "Which one of the following is the most likely diagnosis based on these findings?" Reserve "most clinically significant" for cases where the candidate must rank competing diagnoses by severity, not select a single most-likely.
