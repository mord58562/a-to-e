### Bishop score / induction (files 136-137)
- Lead-in (implied from continuation): "Which one of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?"
- Options: "Artificial rupture of membranes", "Artificial rupture of membranes and oxytocin infusion", "Oxytocin infusion", "Prostaglandin or balloon catheter" (correct, 56 percent), "Caesarean section". Five-option, parallel verb-noun-modifier structure.
- Explanation pattern: "Based on the examination findings, the Bishop score for this patient is 2. Let's break down the scoring." Then explicit enumerated list:
  1. Cervical dilation: 1 cm = 1 point
  2. Cervical effacement: 30 percent = 0 points
  3. Cervical consistency: Firm = 0 points
  4. Cervical position: Posterior = 0 points
  5. Fetal station: -2 = 1 point
  "The total sum is 1 + 0 + 0 + 0 + 1 = 2 points."
- Threshold rule quoted: "A Bishop score of 2 indicates an unfavourable cervix for induction of labour. In such cases, cervical ripening is typically recommended before proceeding with other methods of induction. Prostaglandin or balloon catheter is the most appropriate next step in management."
- Each distractor gets its own bolded heading and a 1-3 sentence "why-not": "Artificial rupture of membranes (ARM) is not recommended as the initial step with an unfavourable cervix..."; "Caesarean section is not indicated at this stage."
- Key learning points (orange/red bullets, 3 of them):
  > "The Bishop score is used to assess cervical readiness for induction of labour, with a score of 6 or more indicating a favourable cervix."
  > "The Bishop score includes five components: cervical dilation, effacement, consistency, position, and fetal station."
  > "A low Bishop score may indicate the need for cervical ripening before proceeding with artificial rupture of membranes."
- Reference number visible: 2.

### Q1 (later in batch) - Betamethasone first at 32w PPROM (files 143-144)
- Stem opener: "A 35-year-old, G2P1, at 32 weeks gestation, presents to the local rural hospital with clear vaginal fluid loss. This occurred one hour ago after she was watching television. - The "first" word in the lead-in is doing the entire pivot, because multiple drugs in the list are arguably indicated. Pure prioritisation Q.
- Explanation opens with definition: "Preterm prelabour rupture of membranes (PPROM) refers to the spontaneous rupture of membranes prior to the 37th week of pregnancy."
- Quoted threshold + reasoning: "The most appropriate medication to administer first is betamethasone (2 doses of 11.4mg, IM, 24 hours apart). ### Q10 - Partosure/Actim Partus testing (files 153-155)
- Stem opener: "A 30-year-old woman, G2P1, presents to the pregnancy assessment unit with lower abdominal cramping at 33 weeks gestation. - Mechanism explanation paragraph: "Preterm labour (PTL) is defined as regular uterine contractions with cervical change (dilatation or length of less than 20 mm on transvaginal ultrasound), or a positive Partosure or Actim Partus qualitative test in a woman between 20 and 36+6 weeks of gestation."
- Then risk-stratification by cm/days: "extremely preterm (less than 28 weeks)", "very preterm (28 to 32 weeks)", "moderate to late preterm (32 to 36+6 weeks)".
- Specific test characteristics: "Once the Partosure swab has been taken, an ultrasound to confirm cephalic presentation is required. Pain relief is often given if the contractions are continuing, nifedipine (20mg PO, every 20mins for 3 doses, then TDS) is used as a tocolytic in the absence of bleeding. - Steroid threshold (28-29+6): "If the Partosure is positive, patients are admitted for observation and prescribed betamethasone (11.4mg IM, 2 doses) to decrease respiratory distress syndrome, hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy (HIE) and necrotising enterocolitis (NEC) as well as decrease NICU admissions in the event of PTB. In an early preterm pregnancy (28-29+6/40), a magnesium infusion for neuroprotection is needed."
- Key learning points (3):
  > "A negative Partosure/Actim Partus has a reliable negative predictive value, and if the tightenings have settled, it may be possible to manage the patient as an outpatient."
  > "Preterm labour is defined as regular uterine contractions with cervical changes between 20 and 36+6 weeks of gestation."
  > "Partosure/Actim Partus testing is useful in assessing the risk of preterm birth within the next seven days."

## Recurring stem patterns

1. Age (years) + parity + gravity (G_P_) + setting ("presents to the [General Practice clinic / Emergency Department / antenatal clinic / labour ward / pregnancy assessment unit / birth suite]") in the opening sentence, with the duration of complaint as a hook ("with a six-week history of", "with a one-hour history of", "with increasing fatigue over several months", "with a six-month history of").
2. Vitals + exam consistently in prose, never tabulated. Format is uniform across the bank: "her pulse rate is X/min, blood pressure X/X mmHg, respiratory rate X/min, SpO2 X percent on room air, and temperature X degrees C."
3. Labs presented as a bulleted list inline (Q5 OGTT, Q21 FBC) with reference range in parentheses immediately after each result. The lab list is the unstructured-prose alternative to a `data_table` field.
4. The crucial discriminator is always the LAST line of the stem (Q4 cord prolapse: "Two hours later, an artificial rupture of membranes is performed and clear amniotic fluid is observed."; Q14: "Recently came home feeling ill from an online provider as part of routine sexual health screening.").
5. Sociocultural anchors are spare and specific (Q21: "originally from Egypt three years ago"; Q3: "She works as a teacher and lives with her partner and three children aged eight, six, and four years."). Ethnicity is volunteered only when it changes the question (Q21 - and then it is a deliberate red herring).
6. Gestational age is always given as weeks (e.g. "32 weeks gestation") or weeks+days ("36+6 weeks"); parity is consistently "G_P_".
7. When the dx-relevant finding is the absence of a feature, the stem says so explicitly ("She has no personal or known family history of venous thromboembolism or cardiovascular disease", Q19; "No gestational sac is visualised", Q16).

## Recurring options patterns

- Always five options across all 24 questions.
- Parallel grammatical structure across the option list (all nouns, all verb-noun, all gestational-age intervals, all timing strings).
- When the question asks about counselling/numerics, options state the number (Q28: "The lifetime risk of pregnancy is approximately 1 in 200"). When the question asks about timing, options state intervals as weeks+days ranges (Q13).
- Distractors include the known textbook wrong answer (Q28 "The risk of ectopic pregnancy is higher than intrauterine pregnancy" - the common board misconception, picked by 56 percent).
- "Reassurance only" appears as a discriminator option in three different stems where over-treatment is the trap (Q11 ruled in by guideline; Q14 ruled out by guideline). Same option text, opposite correct status. The pattern teaches that reassurance is not always the safe default.
- Drug options always include parenthetical dose details in the explanation, not in the option label (Q1 betamethasone is named only as "Betamethasone" in the option but the explanation specifies "2 doses of 11.4mg, IM, 24 hours apart"). Dose-format parity is preserved by NOT putting doses in option text.
- The "physician + procedure" pairing (Q17: "by general gynaecologist" vs "by gynaecological oncologist") is a clean way to test referral decisions inside a single-best-answer.

## Recurring explanation patterns

1. First sentence restates the clinical situation in one line.
2. Definition sentence for the central term ("Preterm prelabour rupture of membranes (PPROM) refers to..."; "Preterm labour (PTL) is defined as..."; "Cord prolapse is a serious obstetrics emergency where...").
3. Mechanism sentence (1-3 sentences).
4. Bolded option name + "is incorrect" or "is correct" + a 2-4 sentence rationale.
5. Closing paragraph that elevates the case to a general principle ("This case highlights the importance of recognising..."; "This case highlights an increasingly common clinical scenario where..."; "In summary, ...").
6. Orange/red highlighted "Key learning points" (1-3 bullets, all written as standalone facts). These are never numbered.
7. Reference count printed at the bottom ("References: 1", "References: 2", "References: 3").
8. Hedging is rare. When present, it is operational, not epistemic ("Failures can occur due to incomplete occlusion, spontaneous recanalisation, or fistula formation").
9. Anti-patterns are surfaced as their own option-aside ("Salbutamol is not indicated as a tocolytic as it has a risk of causing maternal tachycardia as a side effect which can then confuse the clinical picture when monitoring for chorioamnionitis").

## Reusable clinical facts mined

- Bishop score components: cervical dilation, effacement, consistency, position, fetal station. Score >= 6 = favourable. Score 2 = unfavourable, prostaglandin or balloon catheter before ARM/oxytocin.
- Betamethasone dose for fetal lung maturation: 2 x 11.4 mg IM 24 hours apart. Window of optimal benefit: 48 hours to 7 days before delivery, usable up to 34+6/40.
- Erythromycin in PPROM (ORACLE I): decreased delivery rates at 7 days, decreased surfactant administration, decreased positive blood cultures, decreased chronic lung disease and major cerebral haemorrhage; no neonatal harm.
- PPROM definition: spontaneous rupture of membranes before 37+0.
- PPROM in 2.4 percent of singleton births and 7-20 percent of twins; latency >24 hours in 50 percent of women with PPROM but only 20-25 percent at term PROM.
- Preterm labour categories: extremely preterm <28 weeks, very preterm 28-32, moderate-to-late preterm 32-36+6.
- Partosure / Actim Partus: qualitative bedside test for risk of delivery within 7 days. Strong negative predictive value (NPV).
- Tocolysis: nifedipine 20 mg PO every 20 minutes x 3 doses, then TDS.
- MgSO4 neuroprotection: <30 weeks gestation (use in 28-29+6 strongly indicated).
- MCDA twins: deliver 36+0 to 36+6 (per learning bullet) or 37+0 to 37+6 (per scored answer key) - guideline ambiguity flagged.
- DCDA twins: deliver 37+0 to 37+6.
- Bishop score >= 6 = favourable, <6 = ripening agent first.
- Cord prolapse: highest risk with ROM + non-cephalic presentation (especially transverse lie). Immediate caesarean. Manual elevation of presenting part, knee-chest position, fill bladder with 500-700 mL saline.
- Overt diabetes in pregnancy: fasting plasma glucose >= 7.0 mmol/L OR 2-hour 75g OGTT >= 11.1 mmol/L OR HbA1c >= 6.5 percent / 48 mmol/mol OR random plasma glucose >= 11.1 with symptoms.
- Australia GDM screening: 75g OGTT at booking if high-risk, otherwise 24-28 weeks.
- GDM OGTT diagnostic thresholds: fasting >= 5.1, 1-hour >= 10.0, 2-hour >= 8.5 (any one positive).
- Late decelerations on CTG = uteroplacental insufficiency / fetal hypoxia. Persistent late decelerations + reduced variability + meconium = expedited delivery.
- Chorioamnionitis: maternal fever + tachycardia + leucocytosis + raised CRP + fetal tachycardia. Immediate delivery indicated.
- Parvovirus B19 in pregnancy: weekly MCA Doppler USS for 12 weeks to detect fetal anaemia/hydrops. IUT only if hydrops or evidence of severe anaemia. Hydrops typically 2-12 weeks after infection.
- Stress urinary incontinence: pelvic floor exercises first; colposuspension after failed conservative management; oxybutynin/solifenacin/duloxetine are NOT first-line for SUI.
- Endometrial cancer (early stage): laparoscopic TH+BSO by gynaecological oncologist. MRI used to assess myometrial invasion and nodes.
- Ureaplasma asymptomatic carriage: 40-80 percent of sexually active women; not treated; not part of routine STI screening.
- Incomplete miscarriage: heavy bleeding + tissue passage + USS shows retained POC in uterus (heterogeneous material), no gestational sac, may have open or closed cervix.
- HMB definition: blood loss > 80 mL/cycle or > 7 days that interferes with quality of life.
- CA-125 elevated in 80 percent of epithelial ovarian cancers; RMI = CA-125 x menopause status x USS score.
- Cervical screening (Australia 2017+): HPV every 5 years from age 25-74. HPV not 16/18 + pLSIL on reflex LBC = 12-month repeat HPV. HPV 16/18 detected = refer for colposcopy.
- Tubal ligation failure: lifetime risk ~1 in 200 (~0.5 percent over 10 years). Reversal success 40-85 percent depending on maternal age and prior procedure type. Absolute IUP rate exceeds absolute ectopic rate after failure, despite relative risk of ectopic being increased.
- Migraine with aura: absolute contraindication to combined hormonal contraception. Transdermal oestrogen acceptable in perimenopausal migraine because it avoids first-pass hepatic effect and stabilises serum oestrogen.
- Postnatal depression in subsequent pregnancy with stable SSRI: continue current SSRI rather than switch or taper, because of relapse risk.
- Germ cell tumour markers (young woman, complex solid-cystic ovarian mass): AFP, beta-hCG, LDH. CA-125 may be elevated but is not specific.

## Surprising or non-obvious craft choices worth copying

1. **The single-word pivot in lead-in.** Q1's lead-in is "...would be most appropriate to administer first?" The word "first" carries the entire discrimination because the stem contains four drugs that are arguably indicated in PPROM. The whole question becomes a prioritisation drill, not a content recall drill.
2. **Vitals always in prose.** Even with five-line vitals plus exam plus labs, this bank uses prose for vitals/exam and only switches to a bullet list for explicit labs with reference ranges. This avoids the "duplicate data" anti-pattern of having vitals in a table and also restating them in the stem.
3. **Ethnicity used as RED HERRING, then dismantled in the explanation.** Q21's Egyptian background pulls 24 percent of test-takers to thalassaemia; the rationale carefully says "her previous normal haemoglobin three years ago effectively rules this out". This is high-craft because it teaches that demographic priors are not destiny and the time-stamp on the labs is the real discriminator.
4. **The "reassurance" option flipped between right and wrong.** Q11 (parvovirus): reassurance wrong, weekly USS right. Q14 (ureaplasma): reassurance is the right answer. The bank uses identical option text in opposite-correct positions across the bank, ensuring no test-taker can pattern-match on option string.
5. **Risk-statement options written in colloquial English (Q28).** "Pregnancy is impossible after tubal ligation" vs "The lifetime risk of pregnancy is approximately 1 in 200". This makes the question genuinely test whether the candidate can counsel a patient, not just remember a fact.
6. **Each option in counselling questions is something a real doctor might genuinely say.** No straw-man options. Anti-pattern callouts in the explanation gently correct the textbook misconception.
7. **Distinct dose format inside the explanation paragraph, not the option label.** Q1's option says "Betamethasone" only. The explanation specifies "(2 doses of 11.4 mg, IM, 24 hours apart)". This protects option-length parity.
8. **One-sentence forward-pointer at the end of the stem.** Q21: "Previous blood tests from three years ago showed a haemoglobin greater than 110 g/L." Single line dismantles thalassaemia. Q11: "There is a faint lacy rash on her arms and trunk."
9. **"In context" / general-principle paragraph at the end of every explanation.** Phrasings: "This case highlights an increasingly common clinical scenario...", "This case highlights the importance of recognising...", "In summary, ...".
10. **Reference number visible without citations.** Reduces clutter while signalling source-checked content.
11. **Apparent guideline-drift acknowledged in option key vs key-learning bullet.** Q13 MCDA delivery: bullet says "36+0 to 36+6"; key says "37+0 to 37+6". This is a feature, not a bug - real guidelines drift, and the bank shows the candidate that the rule can move.

## Transferable principles (any-topic)

1. **Open with age + setting + duration in one sentence**, no scene-setting words, no transitions. Get to the complaint within 25 words.
2. **Prose for vitals/exam; bullet list with ref ranges for labs**, never duplicate them, never use a table for vitals if prose suffices.
3. **The discriminator is the last line of the stem**, often a single short sentence that flips the diagnosis.
4. **Five options, all parallel grammar, no dose/length tells**. Numeric facts go in the explanation, not the option label, unless the question is explicitly about the number.
5. **Lead-in is one neutral question.** "Which one of the following is the most appropriate ___?" Pick the noun (management plan, next step, diagnosis, most likely cause, most concerning finding, highest risk).
6. **Use single-word pivots ("first", "now", "before delivery") to test prioritisation rather than recognition** when multiple options are credible.
7. **Demographics are weapons, not decoration.** Volunteer ethnicity, occupation, family composition only when they make a true difference; design at least one question per topic where the demographic is the red herring and the explanation explicitly defuses it.
8. **Reassurance must be a live correct AND a live wrong option across the bank.** Flip the polarity intentionally so candidates cannot pattern-match.
9. **Always include the textbook misconception as a high-percentage distractor.** Track its pull rate as a quality metric.
10. **Explanation structure: restate scenario -> define term -> mechanism -> option-by-option -> general-principle close -> key learning bullets (1-3, neutral standalone facts) -> reference count.**
11. **Anti-patterns belong in the option-aside, not the stem.** Never put author meta-commentary or "this distractor hooks X" language in the stem.
12. **Hedging is for operational uncertainty, not epistemic uncertainty.** "Failures can occur due to..." is fine; "It is believed that..." is not.
13. **AU guideline references are named explicitly** (RANZCOG, ASHM, RACGP, ORACLE I trial, ADIPS for diabetes thresholds). Each rationale links to a named source.
14. **One quoted threshold per question is enough.** Use the most actionable numeric: HbA1c >= 6.5 percent, 75g OGTT 2-hour >= 11.1, weekly USS for 12 weeks, deliver 36+0 to 36+6, 1 in 200 lifetime tubal failure.
15. **Counselling questions need plausible-doctor-speech options.** "1 in 200" beats "0.5 percent" when the patient-facing register is being tested.
16. **Avoid dose-format inconsistencies inside the option list.** If one option specifies route + frequency, all must (or none).
17. **End every explanation with the general principle.** The rationale should land somewhere broader than this patient, so the candidate carries away a transferable rule.
18. **Track candidate-percent on each option.** Treat anything > 20 percent on a wrong option as a feature (calibrated distractor) provided the explanation explicitly resolves the trap. Treat anything > 50 percent on a wrong option as a quality flag (Q28, 56 percent wrong, is a teachable moment but worth reviewing).
19. **Reference number > inline citation when display real estate is tight.** Move full sources to a separate pane.
20. **Hold dose / score / threshold facts to the explanation body, not the key learning points.** Bullets should be propositional generalisations, not numerics.
