### Q7 paeds, 1.05.21/27/29 - anaphylaxis
Stem: "An 11-year-old girl presents to the rural GP-run Emergency Department when she experienced sudden onset of dyspnea and noisy breathing at a family gathering. Her medical history includes asthma. She is taking regular inhaled corticosteroids with poor adherence, as well as short-acting beta agonists as needed. She has no known allergies and her immunisations are up to date. On examination, she appears unwell. Her pulse rate is 124/min, blood pressure 76/55 mmHg, respiratory rate 30/min, and oxygen saturation 90% on room air. There is audible wheeze and a persistent cough."
Lead-in: "Which one of the following is the most crucial step in management?"
Options 5, dose-formatted: "Administer X mg dexamethasone PO" / "Administer 5 mg salbutamol via nebuliser" (correct = "Administer 300 micrograms adrenaline IM" 60%) / "Administer 100% oxygen via face mask" / "Administer normal saline 20 mL/kg IV". Note dose+drug+route consistency; mixed units (mg / micrograms / 100%) reflect real practice.
Craft: explicitly names "anaphylaxis" in the explanation header sentence ("This patient is presenting with signs and symptoms consistent with anaphylaxis...") but the stem withholds "anaphylaxis", giving instead the case definition (systems involved + acute onset).
Explanation includes Anaphylaxis definition paragraph: "Anaphylaxis is a clinical diagnosis made after an acute onset of either: Typical skin features ie urticaria, flushing, angioedema as well as or involvement of the respiratory system, and/or cardiovascular system, and/or persistent severe gastrointestinal symptoms / Cardiovascular systems (hypotension), respiratory symptoms (bronchospasm or upper airway obstruction) where anaphylaxis is possible (even if typical skin features are not present)."
Doses given precisely: "Intramuscular adrenaline is the first line treatment for anaphylaxis. It works by causing vasoconstriction (thereby increasing blood pressure), bronchodilation, and stabilisation of mast cells and basophils, thus counteracting the pathophysiological processes of anaphylaxis. The dose of 300 micrograms (0.3 mg) is appropriate for a child weighing more than 30 kg."
Also: "Administer 100% oxygen via face mask and normal saline 20 mg/kg IV are important supportive measures..." and "Administer 5 mg salbutamol via nebuliser can help treat bronchospasm and wheeze associated with anaphylaxis." KLPs:
  - "Anaphylaxis is a severe and life-threatening allergic reaction that should be treated promptly with the administration of IM adrenaline."
  - "In a situation where the diagnosis of anaphylaxis is uncertain, anaphylaxis should be assumed first, even if the treatment of both is time-critical."

## Recurring stem patterns

- **Opening sentence formula**: `"A {age} {year(s)/month/week/day}-old {boy/girl/man/woman} {presents to / is brought to / is reviewed in} the {General Practice clinic / Emergency Department / Special Care nursery / antenatal clinic / hospital antenatal clinic} {with/by} {parent/mother/partner} {with / due to / after} {primary complaint + duration}."` Three settings dominate: GP clinic, ED, SCN/antenatal. Setting itself is a clue (rural ED implies transfer/CT reasoning; SCN implies neonatal).
- **Time anchors**: always relative ("three days ago", "two weeks ago", "for the past four days", "since this morning", "12 weeks gestation", "at 34 weeks gestation"). Never calendar dates.
- **Past medical/medication line**: "He has no significant medical history and takes no regular medications" appears almost verbatim in ~70% of cases. The few exceptions ("history of asthma...regular ICS with poor adherence", "history of opioid use disorder...methadone maintenance", "Mirena IUD") signal where the discriminator lies.
- **Immunisation line**: "His immunisations are up to date" included whenever infection/meningitis is the relevant differential. Its presence/absence is itself a clue.
- **Vitals are always inline prose**, never tabular for routine cases. Order is fixed: pulse > BP > RR > SpO2 > temp. Numerical anchors with reference ranges only for labs that matter (e.g., "Hb 100 g/dL (110-150)"). When labs come in volume (>~4), they shift to a key-value vertical table inside the stem (Q5 paeds biliary atresia, Q2 obgyn CST).
- **Diagnosis withheld in stem**: every diagnostic question and management question seeds illness-script clues without naming the condition. The condition is named in the first sentence of the explanation: "This neonate likely has...", "This patient is presenting with signs and symptoms consistent with...", "This case presents a classic picture of...". The lead-in then carries the appropriate verb (most likely diagnosis / most appropriate next step / most useful investigation).
- **Anti-telegraph substitutions**: "sausage-shaped mass" -> "long, thin mass in the RUQ"; "photophobia" -> "becomes irritable when the room light is turned on"; "currant jelly stools" -> "bloody stools and mucus"; "tea-coloured urine" -> "dark coloured urine"; "café-au-lait" never used outright; "lethargy + neck stiffness" -> "flat and lethargic ... becomes irritable when the room light is turned on" with no neck-stiffness wording at all.
- **Examination block** is its own paragraph, always opens "On examination, ..." with a global gestalt ("he appears unwell" / "she appears well" / "the neonate appears well-nourished but is irritable"). Then vitals. Then targeted exam findings.
- **Social/feeding/development**: a one-line summary when relevant ("He is exclusively breastfed and has been feeding well", "The child has no significant medical history and is up to date with her immunisations"). Social-determinants seeded specifically when discriminator depends on context (Q10 scabies: "They live in a house with 12 occupants in total over three generations. Approximately 10 close-contact paediatric peers").
- **No race or ethnicity** observed in this batch; no Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander references in either paeds or obgyn questions. (Suggests emedici either avoids ethnicity or does not use it as a discriminator. Worth flagging because our bank likely should where epidemiology genuinely depends on it.)
- **Stems include the disconfirming finding to force reasoning**: Q9 obgyn PPH "uterus is contracted, indicating that uterine atony is not the primary cause"; Q5 obgyn neonatal jaundice "DAT negative" + matching ABO + "indirect bilirubin 40 umol/L"; Q9 paeds IgA "throat erythematous with enlarged tonsils" + concurrent haematuria forces synpharyngitic vs post-strep. This is a key craft move.

## Recurring lead-in patterns

Exact phrasings observed:
- "Which one of the following is the most likely diagnosis?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate first step in management?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate initial step in management?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate immediate management?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate management plan?"
- "Which one of the following is the most crucial step in management?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate diagnostic investigation?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate next investigation to confirm the suspected diagnosis?"
- "Which one of the following is the most useful investigation?"
- "Which one of the following best describes the underlying mechanism of this arrhythmia?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate ultrasound screening schedule for {condition} in this pregnancy?"
- "Which one of the following is the recommended next step in management?"
- "Which one of the following cardiotocograph patterns would be most suggestive of {finding} in this case?"
- "Which one of the following statements would be appropriate patient education regarding this skin condition?"

Observations:
- Always single-best ("which one of the following").
- Never two-part. Never "EXCEPT" / negative lead.
- Lead-in is the only stylistic control of difficulty: "first step", "next step", "initial step", "most appropriate", "most useful", "most crucial", "best describes the underlying mechanism" each cue a different cognitive operation.
- "Choose the single best answer" prompt sits between lead-in and options as a small grey instruction.
- Subspecialty-specific lead-ins are present (CTG pattern, ultrasound screening schedule, patient education) - the bank uses these to keep questions in domain even when the underlying skill is generic decision-making.

## Recurring options patterns

- **Count**: 5 options is the dominant default. Q1 obgyn TTTS shows 5 options - all visible after scroll - so I will treat 5 as universal.
- **Parallel grammar**: noun phrases predominantly. When the answer is a drug, all options are dose+drug+route±frequency in identical order: "Administer X mg/microgram drug via route". Mixed units across options ("5 mg salbutamol", "300 micrograms adrenaline", "20 mL/kg normal saline") - emedici intentionally keeps clinically correct units rather than enforcing a single unit, even though that creates length asymmetry. We should consider whether to imitate.
- **Length parity**: options are mostly within ~2-4 words of each other except where adding necessary clinical specificity (eg "Continuous pulse oximetry and supplemental oxygen administration" vs "Administration of surfactant"). Where length differs noticeably the longer option is **not always** the correct one (Q6 MAS correct = longer; Q5 obgyn correct = shorter "Sinusoidal pattern"). This is intentional anti-length-cueing.
- **Ordering**: Often *not* alphabetic. Often a logical-clinical order with most-conservative-to-most-invasive (Q4 paeds haemangioma: Reassurance, Beta blockers, Punch biopsy, Shave biopsy, Surgical excision - escalating invasiveness; Q9 paeds jaundice: phototherapy, urso, surgery referral, biopsy, reassurance - mixed). Sometimes alphabetic (Q8 paeds dx: Septic arthritis, Transient synovitis, Osteomyelitis, Legg-Calve-Perthes, Slipped femoral epiphysis - not alphabetic; appears to be ordered by frequency of the differential).
- **Distractor quality**: every distractor is a clinically real alternative that a candidate could plausibly choose; no filler. Even joke-tier options like "Fumigation is required" in scabies have a clinical-myth rationale.
- **Negation absent**: no "Do not X" options. No "no treatment required" generic option.
- **AU dose-format conventions observed**:
  - micrograms spelled out, not 'mcg' or 'µg' (correct AU practice)
  - "mg" and "mg/kg" both used; weight-based vs absolute used appropriately
  - "IV" and "IM" and "PO" capitals
  - "g" used for large doses (cefotaxime 2 g)
  - "/min" not "bpm" or "per minute"
  - "mmHg" tight, no spaces
  - Spelled out drug names (no brand names)
- **One correct shown with green tick + "Best Answer" pill (teal)**; selected-but-incorrect with red ring + "Your Answer" pill (red).

## Recurring explanation patterns

- **First sentence**: states the diagnosis + brief justification. "This neonate likely has meconium aspiration syndrome (MAS)." / "This patient is presenting with signs and symptoms consistent with anaphylaxis..." / "This case presents a classic picture of acute osteomyelitis, typically..." / "This patient's presentation is highly suggestive of Guillain-Barre syndrome (GBS), an acute inflammatory polyneuropathy often triggered by a preceding infection."
- **Per-option paragraph**: each option (correct first, then distractors in the same order as displayed) gets a paragraph with the option name bolded and explanation of why correct/wrong. Internal structure is consistent: option-bold + "is/may be considered..." + clinical reasoning + why it is or is not appropriate here. Distractor paragraphs explicitly explain when the distractor *would* be correct - this is excellent pedagogy.
- **Hedge phrasing for first option**: "would be appropriate" / "may be considered" / "is reasonable in this context"; for outright wrong distractors: "would not be necessary" / "is not indicated" / "would be inappropriate". Never says "wrong" directly.
- **Length**: explanations are long; typically ~250-500 words across visible scrolls. They are deliberately verbose to reach all the teaching points implicit in the case (not just the answer).
- **Key Learning Points block**: bottom of explanation, orange-highlighted bullets. Always 2-3 KLPs. Each starts with the diagnosis/condition + the action statement. Worded as portable rules, not case-specific commentary.
- **"In context" or "In this case" pattern**: many explanations have an "In this case..." paragraph mid-text that applies the general principle back to the patient. Eg Q7 anaphylaxis: "In this case, the patient is showing the typical skin features, lowered prevalence with hypotension..."
- **References**: every explanation has a "References" footer with a chevron indicating expandable citations. Sources are numbered.
- **No markdown** in the actual rendered explanation - it appears to be HTML with bolds.
- **No "*Why the others are not*" header section** - the per-option discussion is inline narrative rather than a labelled subsection. This is *less* structured than our current format. We should keep our explicit section.
- **Differential discrimination**: explanations explicitly discriminate the correct dx vs the most tempting alternate (e.g., MAS vs pneumonia; volvulus vs SBO atresia; pericarditis vs myocarditis; IgA vs PSGN; osteomyelitis vs septic arthritis vs transient synovitis). The discrimination paragraph is structured: "X can present similarly to Y, however..."

## Surprising or non-obvious craft choices worth copying

1. **Anti-telegraph paraphrasing**: emedici systematically rewrites textbook phrases as plausible clinical descriptions ("becomes irritable when the room light is turned on" for photophobia; "long, thin mass in the RUQ" for sausage-shaped). Adopt strictly in our bank.
2. **Disconfirming finding seeded in stem to force reasoning past default**: "the uterus is contracted, indicating uterine atony is not the primary cause" or "DAT negative... matched blood groups". This single technique is the dominant difficulty lever and we should imitate it.
3. **Lead-in as the only difficulty lever**: "most appropriate first step" vs "most appropriate next step" vs "most useful investigation" vs "most crucial step" vs "best describes the underlying mechanism" - identical scenario can ramp 2-3 difficulty levels purely by changing the lead-in verb.
4. **The "irrelevant-but-true" red herring**: GBS prophylaxis history in MAS case; opioid use disorder/methadone in hypoglycaemia case; recent URTI in scabies/Meckel case. Each is true and contextually consistent but does not change first-step management. This is much harder to engineer than fake distractors but is the difference between 4/5 and 5/5.
5. **Distractor paragraph explains *when* the distractor would be correct**: gives the candidate the "if-then" rule for next encounter (educational ROI is huge).
6. **Sources displayed but identity-obscured below explanation**: keeps explanation uncluttered; expandable on click. Good UX.
7. **No "X-style" or "according to local guidelines, choose..."** lead-ins. Lead-ins are clinical not regulatory.
8. **Per-option cohort %** is displayed *during/after* answering - exposes discrimination value directly (Q9 paeds osteo had 51% correct + 46% septic arthritis; Q1 obgyn TTTS 38% correct + 27% wrong-window). emedici uses these to identify good items. Worth emulating offline (our analytics already track this but we should surface it for self-audit).
9. **Examination paragraph starts with global gestalt** ("appears well" / "appears unwell" / "is flat and lethargic") - this single phrase is itself a clue and is consistent across every Q.
10. **No two-part questions** observed. Single best answer only. We should hold that line.
11. **Setting selection (GP vs ED vs SCN vs antenatal clinic) is meaningful**, not arbitrary - GP cases ask conservative/refer; ED cases ask immediate; SCN cases ask neonatal-specific. Setting itself reduces the answer space.
12. **Numbers always with reference ranges in parens for paeds labs**; AU units (g/L, mmol/L, U/L, micromol/L) consistent; never just "180" without units.
13. **Inclusion of a global "she has used condoms irregularly" or "12 occupants in the household"** to anchor risk where it is the discriminator. Adopt for psych (housing, supports) and surgery (occupation, return to work).
14. **The stem often ends mid-investigation** ("Initial ultrasound shows malposition of the SMA"), forcing the candidate to pick the *next* step rather than the gestalt diagnosis.
15. **Vital signs that are normal are still reported** ("his vital signs are within normal limits" / "pulse 95/min, BP 110/70, RR 16/min, SpO2 98%, temp 36.7"). Cuts a class of cheap distractors and tests whether the candidate can ignore them.

## Transferable principles (applicable to any topic - medicine, psych, surgery, etc.)

1. **Withhold the diagnosis from the stem; let the illness script carry it.** The lead-in stays neutral; the explanation names the diagnosis in sentence 1. Applies equally to psychiatry (don't name "depression with melancholic features"), surgery, internal medicine.
2. **Paraphrase telegraph phrases.** Build an explicit ban list per condition (your own version of mine): for each high-yield dx, list the 3-5 textbook phrases never to use, with paraphrases. Apply uniformly.
3. **Difficulty rides on the lead-in verb, not stem length or rare topic.** "Most appropriate first step" / "Most appropriate next step" / "Most useful investigation" / "Best describes the mechanism" / "Most appropriate education statement" all change the cognitive operation while preserving the underlying clinical scenario. This is our chief difficulty lever - aligns with Rob's reasoning-is-the-only-lever rule.
4. **Seed disconfirming findings to force reasoning past default.** Explicitly negate the textbook-default cause ("uterus contracted, not atony"; "DAT negative, blood groups matched"; "vital signs within normal limits"). Forces candidate to reason past first-pass.
5. **Set the scene through location.** GP / ED / SCN / antenatal clinic / acute psych unit / OT / community mental-health team / nursing home each scopes the answer set. Choose deliberately.
6. **Relative time anchors only.** Never calendar dates. Always "three days ago", "two weeks ago", "since this morning", "for the past 6 months", "at 12 weeks gestation".
7. **Vitals/labs in prose unless many; switch to vertical table at >=5 labs or >=4 vitals with values.** Reference ranges in parens.
8. **Distractors must be real clinical alternatives, each with a context in which they would be correct.** The explanation paragraph for each distractor names that context.
9. **Each option's bold name + paragraph in the explanation**, in the same order as displayed. This is a stronger pedagogy pattern than "Why correct + Why others wrong" because it respects display order.
10. **2-3 portable KLPs in highlighted bullets at the bottom**, each containing the condition name + the rule. They should read as decontextualised rules ready for spaced recall.
11. **Anti-length-cueing audit**: shortest answer must occasionally be correct; longest answer must occasionally be wrong. Audit per batch.
12. **Disconfirming exam findings are the cheapest 4/5 lever** (atony ruled out before PPH Q; ROM preserved before bone pain Q). Practice writing the stem from the *distractor's* perspective and adding one sentence that kills it.
13. **"In context" paragraph** applies the general rule back to the patient. Builds the bridge between rote and clinical reasoning.
14. **Use settings, immunisation status, social structure (household, occupation, supports) as discriminator anchors**, not as filler. Where they appear, they matter. Where they don't matter, omit.
15. **AU-specific cutoffs are the discrimination point** in many questions (CST algorithm, NIPS, ASCIA chart, RCH CPG dosing). Mining published AU guideline cutoff numbers and engineering one question per cutoff is a high-yield generation strategy.
16. **Patient education / counselling lead-ins** are an underused difficulty lever - they let you test the same condition from a different angle (transmission, prognosis, follow-up timing, expected course post-treatment). Adopt sparingly.
17. **Mechanism-of-disease lead-ins** ("best describes the underlying mechanism of this arrhythmia") let you test pathophysiology without writing a different stem. High reuse value.
18. **No two-part questions, no negative lead-ins, no "EXCEPT"**, no markdown asterisks in stems, no leading meta-commentary - these are all consistent with our existing audit rules. emedici confirms our rule set.

## Notes on what emedici does that we should NOT copy

- Mixed-unit option dose formatting (e.g., "300 micrograms" vs "5 mg" vs "20 mL/kg") - we should pick a single dose-format convention within an option set even if real-world units differ, because dose-format parity is a Rob-binding audit rule.
- Inline narrative discrimination instead of explicit "Why the others are not" section - we have a stronger format with explicit sections; keep ours.
- "Best Answer" / "Your Answer" labels - cosmetic; we already do this differently.
- Cohort percentages in the option row are useful but cluttered; ours behind a click would be cleaner.
- "References [count]" expander is fine but we should keep our citation visible by default per the Rob feedback rule that every rationale links to a cited source by name.
