### Q23 - NSAIDs in pregnancy 20 weeks
- Stem: "A 27 year-old primigravida woman presents for her general practitioner following two positive urine pregnancy tests. She has a regular 28 days cycle and her last menstrual period two weeks ago. She is happy with her pregnancy however she is worried regarding the effects that some of her medications might have on the foetus. Her medical history includes major depression, iron deficiency anaemia, and epilepsy managed by levetiracetam. She also takes sertraline, iron tablets, and occasional ibuprofen and paracetamol for tension headaches. Her over the counter medications include vitamin D supplements during winter and daily vitamin B12."
- Lead-in: "Which one of the following medications should be discontinued during pregnancy?"
- Options: Ibuprofen [correct] / Paracetamol / Sertraline / Iron tablets / Levetiracetam.
- Multi-drug discussion: "NSAIDs is contraindicated during pregnancy. Early closure of ductus arteriosus and renal toxicity in the foetus is associated with use of non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDS) during >20 weeks of gestation. This is thought to occur due to the inhibition of prostaglandin, leading to premature closure of ductus arteriosus."
- "Iron tablets and paracetamol are generally safe to take during pregnancy. Repeat blood test and iron studies should be done to determine the severity of her iron deficiency anaemia. Dosing of iron tablets should be adjusted according to the results of her investigations."
- "Sertraline is a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI). It is safe antidepressant to use during pregnancy. Although the use of sertraline during the second and third trimester is thought to be associated with lower birth weight and mild respiratory distress in newborn, pregnancy is not an absolute contraindication for prescribing sertraline."
- "Levetiracetam is epilepsy management should be continued during pregnancy. Uncontrolled seizure can be life threatening for the mother and foetus, therefore most epileptic patients are recommended to continue on at least one epileptic drug. Levetiracetam is an anti-epileptic drug with no known teratogenicity, and is usually the recommended anti-epileptic drug to use during pregnancy. It can also be continued when the mother is lactating. It is important to prescribe a higher dose of folate supplements (5 mg) for pregnant women with epilepsy diagnosis. Sodium valproate/carbamazepine should also be avoided during pregnancy for the risk of neural tube defects in the newborn."
- Key learning: "NSAIDs use during >20 weeks of gestation is associated with early closure of ductus arteriosus and renal toxicity in fetus."

- "On examination, she appears well with vital signs within normal limits. Her abdomen is soft with mild tenderness in the right iliac fossa. There is no rebound tenderness or guarding."

### Q32 - HPV-16/18 + unsatisfactory LBC

### Q33 - Labour progress - dilatation rate
- Stem: "A 28 year-old woman presents to the labour ward with her first pregnancy at 38 weeks of gestation. She reports regular, painful contractions for the past four hours. - Explanation - careful framing of "average not minimum": "The correct answer is 0.5-1 cm per hour. This represents the average rate of cervical dilation for a primigravida in active labour. - "1.5-2 cm per hour and greater than 2 cm per hour represent more rapid progress than the average expectation for a primigravida. While not necessarily abnormal in the women and foetus are coping well, these rates are significantly faster than typically seen in first-time mothers."
- Key learning: "Normal cervical dilation in active labour is expected to progress at an average rate of 0.5-1 cm per hour for nulliparous women and 1.5 cm per hour for multiparous women / The active phase of labour typically begins at 4-6 cm cervical dilation / Partographs are essential tools for monitoring labour progress and identifying deviations from normal patterns."

### Q34 - Caesarean scar ectopic pregnancy
- Stem: "A 32 year-old woman presents to the Emergency Department with weeks of amenorrhoea and three days of light vaginal bleeding. She has a medical history of two previous caesarean sections and takes no regular medications. She reports HRT. On examination, she appears uncomfortable but stable. Her pulse rate is 78/min, blood pressure 120/75 mmHg, respiratory rate 16/min, SpO2 98% on room air, and temperature 36.6 deg C. Her abdomen is soft and non-tender. Speculum examination reveals a closed cervical os with minimal dark blood in the vaginal vault."
- "Serum beta human chorionic gonadotropin (bHCG) is 12,400 IU/L (5-50). Transvaginal ultrasound shows an empty endometrial cavity with normal endometrial thickness. A 6 week gestational sac containing a yolk sac and 2.5 mm foetal pole is visualised in the anterior aspect of the lower uterine segment. The myometrium between the gestational sac and the bladder measures 2 mm in thickness. Colour Doppler demonstrates increased vascularity surrounding the gestational sac. Cardiac activity is present with a foetal heart rate of 145 beats/min."
- Lead-in: "Which one of the following is the most likely diagnosis?"
- Options: Caesarean scar ectopic pregnancy [correct] / Cervical ectopic pregnancy / Threatened miscarriage / Low implantation pregnancy / Cornual ectopic pregnancy.
- Discriminator key: "myometrium between the gestational sac and the bladder measures 2 mm" plus "anterior aspect of the lower uterine segment" - exact features.
- Explanation: "Caesarean scar ectopic pregnancy occurs when a blastocyst implants into the myometrial defect of a previous caesarean scar. The incidence is increasing with the rising caesarean section rate worldwide. Transvaginal ultrasound is the gold standard for diagnosis, with key features including an empty uterine cavity, gestational sac located anteriorly at the level of the internal os in the site of the previous caesarean section scar, and thin or absent myometrium between the gestational sac and bladder. This condition carries significant risks including uterine rupture, life-threatening haemorrhage, and loss of future fertility if not diagnosed and managed appropriately."
- Each other option dispatched: "Cervical ectopic pregnancy involves implantation of the gestational sac in the endocervical canal. While it can present similarly with vaginal bleeding and a positive pregnancy test, on ultrasound the gestational sac would be located within the cervical canal below the level of the internal os, with the uterine cavity empty. The hourglass shape of the uterus with ballooning of the cervix is characteristic."
- Key learning: "Caesarean scar ectopic pregnancy is characterised by implantation of a gestational sac in the myometrial defect of a previous caesarean scar / Transvaginal ultrasound is the gold standard for diagnosing a caesarean scar ectopic pregnancy / Key diagnostic features include an empty uterine cavity, gestational sac implanted in the anterior lower uterine segment at the site of previous caesarean scar, and thin or absent myometrium."

### Q36 - Contraception and BMI 36
- Stem: "A 38-year-old woman, G3P3, presents to the General Practice clinic requesting contraception. She is an ex-smoker with a 10 pack year history having quit eight months ago. She has no other significant medical history and does not take any regular medications. Her body mass index is 36 kg/m2. On examination, she appears well with vital signs within normal limits. She prefers oral contraception and is reliable with taking daily medications. She declines long-acting reversible contraception as she is uncomfortable with implanted devices."
- Lead-in: "Which one of the following is the most appropriate statement regarding contraceptive options for this patient?"

- Critical AU framework cited: "This patient requires contraception in the context of obesity (BMI 36 kg/m2) and a history of smoking (quit >1 year). The UK Medical Eligibility Criteria for Contraceptive Use (UKMEC) categorises risk from 1 (no restriction) to 4 (unacceptable health risk). Progesterone-only pill is a safe option in this context. The progestogen-only pill (POP) is classified as UKMEC Category 1 for women with a BMI >=35 kg/m2 (Category 2 if smoker of any age), it is not associated with an increased risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE) or cardiovascular disease, making it the most appropriate and safest oral option for this patient."
- "Combined oral contraceptive pill is a safe option is incorrect. The combined contraceptive pill (COCP) is classified as UKMEC Category 3 for women with a BMI >=35 kg/m2, meaning the risks usually outweigh the benefits. Furthermore, guidelines highlight that the presence of multiple risk factors for arterial or venous disease (especially combined with smoking) compounds risk. Even though her smoking history alone (stopped >1 year) is Category 3, the combination with obesity creates a cumulative risk profile that reinforces the contraindication for combined hormonal contraception products."

While DMPA is a useful method, DMPA is classified as UKMEC Category 2 for women with a BMI >=35 kg/m2 due to concerns regarding weight gain and potential cardiovascular effects. When multiple cardiovascular risk factors are present (e.g. obesity plus smoking), the UKMEC suggests the category may escalate to Category 3, making it a less favourable choice than the POP in this patient."
- Key learning: "Progesterone-only pills are UKMEC Category 1 for women with a BMI >=35 kg/m2 and do not increase the risk of venous thromboembolism / Combined oral contraceptive pills are UKMEC Category 3 for women with a BMI >=35 kg/m2 representing a risk that usually outweighs the benefits / The presence of multiple risk factors for cardiovascular disease or venous thromboembolism often escalates the medical eligibility category, contraindicating combined hormonal contraception products."

### Q37 - Cholecystitis in pregnancy 30 weeks
- Stem: "A 27 year-old woman, G1P0, presents to the Emergency Department with a 24-hour history of increasing right upper quadrant pain associated with nausea. She is currently in good health, with no significant medical or surgical history. She is currently 30 weeks pregnant. On examination she looks well, with a blood pressure of 110/70 mmHg, pulse 90/min and temperature 37.6C. She is tender immediately under the right costal margin. - Explanation: "It was previously taught that semi-elective abdominal surgery should be delayed until the postpartum period due to the perceived risk of surgical and obstetric complications (including preterm labour) however this approach is associated with increased patient discomfort, lower patient satisfaction and increased presentations to hospital for management of the same symptoms. Whilst it is still not uncommon to see pregnant women with an acute abdomen related to acute cholecystitis/ recurrent cholelithiasis managed conservatively on the ward, there are a multitude of studies supporting the safety of laparoscopic surgery for cholecystitis and appendicitis in pregnancy. ## Recurring stem patterns

- Opening sentence ALWAYS: "A [age] year-old [woman/man/boy/girl] presents to the [General Practice clinic / Emergency Department / hospital antenatal clinic / labour ward] with [duration + symptom]." Never deviates.
- Demographic block: age, gravidity/parity (G_P_) where relevant, sometimes ethnicity ("Aboriginal woman from a remote community in the Northern Territory"), occupation occasionally ("She works as a bus driver"), social factors ("ex-smoker with a 10 pack year history having quit eight months ago", "in a same-sex relationship with another woman", "five sexual partners in the last year").
- PMHx and meds: prose, almost always paired sentences. "She has no significant medical history and takes no regular medications." is a near-template.
- Vitals always: pulse rate / blood pressure / respiratory rate / SpO2 / temperature - in that exact order, in prose, units explicit (/min, mmHg, deg C). Pulse first never blood pressure.
- Labs almost always woven into prose with reference ranges in parentheses: "CA-125: 450 U/mL (0-35)", "ALT 70 U/L (<55)", "haemoglobin 130 g/L (115-160)".
- Imaging description first lists features ("hyperechoic material, calcification, and fluid-fluid level"), location ("anterior aspect of the lower uterine segment"), measurements ("a 6 week gestational sac containing a yolk sac and 2.5 mm foetal pole"). Never names the diagnosis ("dermoid cyst", "caesarean scar ectopic") in the stem.
- Withheld content: dx withheld; sometimes the discriminator value is named but its significance unstated (e.g. "myometrium 2 mm" - reader must know that <5 mm = caesarean scar ectopic).
- Stems are MEDIUM length (typically 110-180 words). Never under 80, rarely over 200.
- Vitals stay in stem even when normal - the abnormal one is buried among normals (BP 165/110 in Q29 buried among RR 12 SpO2 100).
- "She does not take any regular medications" repeated near-verbatim is intentional, drops cognitive load to focus discriminator elsewhere.
- Relative-time anchors: "two minutes" (seizure duration), "two months ago" (smear), "eight months ago" (quit smoking), "28 days cycle and her last menstrual period two weeks ago" - always concrete numbers, never "recently".
- Gestation always: "X weeks gestation" or "X+Y weeks gestation" (e.g. "37+2 weeks").
- Foetal HR routinely included in obstetric stems, normalised range 120-160.
- Family history offered explicitly with relation + age + outcome: "Her mother dying suddenly during pregnancy at age 30".
- BMI is given as kg/m2 (with the "2" in line, not superscript) when relevant.
- A few stems intentionally pack quasi-irrelevant social detail ("She is in a same-sex relationship with another woman" in Q32) - these are deliberately neutral, NOT discriminators, training non-leading clinical reasoning.

## Recurring lead-in patterns

- Dominant lead-in: "Which one of the following is the most appropriate [next step in management / first step in management / immediate medication / management plan / advice regarding the pregnant mother]?"
- Alt for dx questions: "Which one of the following is the most likely diagnosis?"
- For knowledge/truth: "Which one of the following is the most accurate statement regarding [X]?" or "Which of the following correctly describes [X]?"
- For ruling-IN tests: "A positive result in which one of the following investigations is most likely to influence management of this patient's underlying condition?" (Q28) - a beautiful non-leading framing that doesn't telegraph "pregnant" or "syphilis".
- For management options where each option is a discrete statement: "Which one of the following is the most appropriate statement regarding [contraceptive options / management of her epilepsy] for this patient?"
- For investigations selectivity: "With regards to the [CT findings], which one of the following is most appropriate?"
- Lead-ins are STRICTLY neutral. Never "What is the best next step?" without qualifier. Always "the most appropriate" not "the best".
- Never "Choose all that apply". Always single-best-answer ("Choose the single best answer" appears as italic under the lead-in).
- No negative lead-ins observed in this batch except Q23 "Which one of the following medications should be discontinued during pregnancy?" - which is positively framed (discontinue) not "should NOT be continued".

## Recurring options patterns

- ALWAYS 5 options.
- Parallel structure rigorously enforced: all are verbs (start X / transfer to Y / refer for Z) OR all noun phrases (ergometrine / misoprostol / blood products / oxytocin / carboprost) OR all dx labels. Never mixed.
- For management plans, options often span the SAME axis (time-to-rescreen: 6 weeks / 6 months / 1 year) plus one over-treatment plus one correct procedure (Q7).
- For dx, options chosen from a tight differential: same organ-system or same syndrome family.
- Dose options when present are formatted identically: dose + unit + frequency (no mismatch).
- Distractor quality: each is a real consideration that requires specific knowledge to rule out. Not throwaway. E.g. Q12 - all 5 syndromes can cause chest pain in pregnancy; the chosen-correct vs Marfan is distinguished by family hx of sudden death + skin hyperextensibility.
- Options never reveal the diagnosis when the stem withheld it (Q26 - options listed include "Ultrasound follow-up", "Serum CA125", etc., none name "dermoid").
- Percentages displayed next to each option in italic showing user-choice distribution (e.g. "Reassurance and retest CST in 1 year 7%"). The correct answer is flagged "Real Answer" in a green pill. This is the platform UI, not part of the question.
- Options ordered: not strictly alphabetical, not strictly by acuity. Often ordered to put the correct answer in the middle (3rd or 4th position) - psychological neutrality.

## Recurring explanation patterns

Standard structure observed in essentially every Q:

1. Opening paragraph: re-states what the patient presents with + names the dx ("This patient presents with [X]"). NEVER hedges with "likely". Confident dx commitment.
2. Mechanism paragraph: explains pathophysiology in 3-5 sentences. Often cites prevalence numbers ("approximately 20% of all ovarian neoplasms").
3. Each non-correct option discussed by name in BOLD: "Cervical ectopic pregnancy involves implantation of..." - one paragraph each. Each gets the same length treatment for parity.
4. Guideline reference inserted parenthetically with name: "(Consensus recommendation 6.48 - Colposcopy referral for atypical glandular/endocervical cells)", "The Royal Australian and New Zealand College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists (RANZCOG)", "UK Medical Eligibility Criteria for Contraceptive Use (UKMEC)".
5. "In context" or closing paragraph linking back to AU practice / next step / multidisciplinary care.
6. Yellow-block "Key learning point" of 1-3 bullet-style sentences. Almost always: (a) the chosen pathway in the specific patient, (b) the broader rule, (c) the workup principle.

Other explanation observations:
- Tables embedded in explanations when comparing drugs (Q16 - Valproate / Phenytoin / Carbamazepine + side effects).
- Numbers cited freely: "500 mL", ">23 weeks", ">35 kg/m2", "CA-125 elevated in 80%", "20% of ovarian neoplasms".
- Mechanism is always tied to dx ("Magnesium acts through multiple mechanisms: it is a calcium antagonist..."), not generic.
- "Why-others-are-wrong" sections add NEW information about each option, not a restatement of why the correct one is correct.
- AU specifics injected naturally: "in remote Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities in Northern Australia", "Australia has a national cervical screening programme (NCSP)".
- Source citations appear inside parens: "(Consensus recommendation 6.48 - Colposcopy referral...)", "RANZCOG", "UKMEC", "NCSP". No academic-style citations; instead the guideline body name + recommendation number where applicable.
- Length: explanations typically 250-400 words. Long but not bloated; every sentence does work.
- "Reference" link appears at the bottom of each explanation (icon + number).

## Reusable clinical facts mined

Cervical screening / HPV / colposcopy:
- HPV non-16/18 + LBC pLSIL: repeat CST in 12 months.
- HPV non-16/18 + atypical glandular cells (any LBC): refer for colposcopy (high-risk category due to adenocarcinoma risk).
- HPV 16/18 detected (any LBC): refer for colposcopy regardless of LBC.
- Self-collected sample: only HPV tested, no reflex LBC. If HPV 16/18 -> direct colposcopy; if HPV non-16/18 -> clinician-collected sample for reflex LBC.
- Cervical co-test recommended at 6 months only for intermediate-risk pathway, not as initial screen after positive result.
- Treatment after colposcopy + HSIL on biopsy: LLETZ.
- Surveillance after LLETZ: test of cure at 6 mo, then HPV every 12 mo until two consecutive negatives, then return to 5-yearly.

PPH:
- PPH = >=500 mL; severe PPH = >=1,000 mL; major haemorrhage = >=2,500 mL.
- 4 Ts: tone, trauma, tissue (retained products), thrombin.
- Genital tract trauma with active bleeding: direct pressure to bleeding site is first step before uterotonics.
- Oxytocin: first-line uterotonic for atony.
- Ergometrine: contraindicated in HTN, pre-eclampsia (risk of stroke, coronary spasm).
- Carboprost: 3rd-line.
- Tranexamic acid: adjunct.
- Bakri balloon: tamponade for ongoing atony.
- Definitive surgical repair in OT for genital tract trauma.

Late TOP:
- Late TOP >23 weeks: feticide first (intracardiac KCl under USS guidance), then medical induction with mifepristone + misoprostol.
- Mife + miso alone (without feticide) inappropriate >23 weeks because risk of live birth.
- Caesarean delivery for TOP: never first line; reserved for maternal indication.

Anti-D (NBA-aligned):
- All RhD-negative women without preformed antibodies receive routine anti-D 625 IU at 28 and 34 weeks gestation.
- 625 IU also postpartum if baby is RhD positive.
- Sensitising events triggering anti-D: miscarriage, CVS, amniocentesis, abdominal trauma, ECV, antepartum haemorrhage.
- 250 IU for sensitising events in 1st trimester; 625 IU for sensitising events >12 weeks.
- Non-sensitising: NIPT, live vaccines (e.g. MMR), routine speculum/digital examination, future blood transfusion (does not sensitise the recipient to RhD via amnio-route mechanism).

Severe pre-eclampsia:
- Severe pre-eclampsia BP cut-off: BP >=160/110 plus end-organ involvement (HELLP, neurological, renal, hepatic, proteinuria/Cr).
- Mg sulphate IV is FIRST-line for eclampsia prevention; both vasodilator and anticonvulsant.
- Fluid restriction 80-100 mL/hr to avoid pulmonary oedema.
- Transfer contraindicated when patient in advanced labour (>=8 cm) - stabilise and deliver locally.

Epilepsy and pregnancy:
- Sodium valproate: teratogen (NTD, skeletal abn, developmental delay); avoid entirely if alternative available.
- Phenytoin: fetal hydantoin syndrome (cleft lip/palate, developmental delay).
- Carbamazepine: fetal hydantoin syndrome, spina bifida.
- Lamotrigine, levetiracetam: preferred AEDs in pregnancy.
- Folic acid 5 mg daily for all women with epilepsy planning pregnancy.
- AED continuation in pregnancy: do NOT stop; uncontrolled seizures are dangerous; fetus tolerates brief hypoxia.
- Fetal bradycardia during GTC may occur and typically self-resolves.

Other drugs in pregnancy:
- NSAIDs (e.g. ibuprofen): avoid >20 weeks due to premature ductus arteriosus closure + renal toxicity.
- Paracetamol: safe.
- SSRIs (sertraline): safe; mild risk of low birth weight + transient resp distress.
- Iron tablets: safe; adjust dose per investigations.

Adnexal masses / ovarian cysts:
- Simple cyst <5 cm in pre-menopausal woman: observation only.
- Simple cyst 5-7 cm: USS follow-up in 2-4 months via GP.
- Simple cyst >=7 cm or with malignant features: refer gynae, consider further imaging + CA-125.
- Dermoid cyst: hyperechoic material + calcification + fluid-fluid level on USS; 20% of all ovarian neoplasms; most common germ cell tumour; reproductive-age woman; risk of torsion.
- Epithelial ovarian cancer: post-menopausal, fixed unilateral mass, raised CA-125; CA-125 elevated in 80%.
- Germ cell ovarian cancer: teens to early-20s, AFP/hCG elevated.
- Risk of Malignancy Index (RMI) = CA-125 + menopausal status + USS features.

Caesarean scar ectopic:
- Empty uterine cavity + sac in anterior LUS at site of prior CS scar + myometrium <5 mm between sac and bladder + increased Doppler vascularity.
- TVUSS = gold standard.
- Differential from cervical ectopic (sac below internal os, hourglass uterus).

Labour progress:
- Active phase begins at 4-6 cm.
- Expected dilatation: 0.5-1 cm/hr in nulliparous; ~1.5 cm/hr in multiparous.
- Partogram for monitoring.

Vascular Ehlers-Danlos:
- Family history of sudden death in young relative + hyperextensibility = think vEDS.
- Significant maternal mortality from arterial rupture in pregnancy.
- Urgent echo for suspected aortic dissection.

Recurrent miscarriage (uterine factor):
- Septate uterus: 3D TVUSS or MRI for dx (single fundus, normal external contour, midline septum).
- Hysteroscopic metroplasty: minimally invasive, RANZCOG-recommended.
- Avoid endometrial ablation (contraindicated in those wanting to conceive).
- Wait 2-3 months after metroplasty to attempt conception.

HFMD in pregnancy:
- Coxsackievirus A16 + enterovirus 71.
- No congenital syndrome / no increased miscarriage risk.
- No specific intervention for pregnant contacts.

Syphilis:
- Secondary syphilis: copper-coloured maculopapular rash on palms and soles + lymphadenopathy + condylomata lata.
- Treponema pallidum.
- AU notifiable; higher rates in remote Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities, esp. NT.
- Pregnancy test is critical-to-management (congenital syphilis = miscarriage, stillbirth, neonatal death, PTL, IUGR).
- Treatment unchanged (benzathine penicillin G), but pregnancy changes follow-up and obstetric referral.

Contraception (UKMEC):
- POP: Cat 1 if BMI >=35 (Cat 2 if also smoker, any age).
- COCP: Cat 3 if BMI >=35; Cat 3 if ex-smoker stopped <1 yr (Cat 2 if >1 yr ago).
- DMPA: Cat 2 if BMI >=35; escalates to Cat 3 with combined CV risk factors.
- Multiple risk factors compound risk; escalate category.

Cholecystitis in pregnancy:
- Laparoscopic cholecystectomy safe through to 3rd trimester.
- Preferred over conservative management (worse outcomes, recurrent presentations).
- Open cholecystectomy not justified; percutaneous drain reserved for unwell patients.

## Surprising or non-obvious craft choices worth copying

1. **Vital signs always in prose, always in same order, even when totally normal.** Forces reader to scan and find the abnormal value rather than spoon-feeding a "flagged" abnormal. This is a meaningful test of vigilance and replicates clinical work.
2. **Reference ranges in parens after every lab value.** No assumption of memory. "(0-35)", "(<55)", "(115-160)". Removes ambiguity about whether a value is abnormal.
3. **Discriminator feature embedded as numerical detail, not as adjective.** "Myometrium between gestational sac and bladder measures 2 mm" not "thin myometrium". Reader must know the threshold (<5 mm).
4. **Family history of sudden death always given with age + relation + outcome.** "Her mother dying suddenly during pregnancy at age 30." Doesn't say "family history of aortic dissection" - that would telegraph.
5. **Stems sometimes include benign social detail that is NOT a discriminator.** "She is in a same-sex relationship with another woman" - trains the reader to NOT use orientation/gender/parity in shortcut reasoning.
6. **Withheld diagnosis even when discriminator is named in options.** "Ultrasound follow-up" / "Laparoscopic cyst removal" - none of the management options name "dermoid".
7. **Lead-in for investigations: "A positive result in which one of the following investigations is most likely to influence management of this patient's underlying condition?"** This is brilliant - non-leading because "underlying condition" doesn't telegraph dx, "influence management" doesn't telegraph which-test-to-rule-in-vs-out.
8. **Each "wrong" option discussed by name in bold in the explanation, given roughly equal length.** Eliminates the "is the right one always the longest paragraph?" tell.
9. **Truth-claim style options for knowledge-axis questions.** Q16, Q19, Q33, Q36 - each option is a complete statement. Forces evaluation of each as a discrete proposition. Excellent for testing nuance.
10. **Calculation embedded in the explanation, not stem.** Q14 - "weighing of soaked pads" calculation (1000g extra). Reader uses the stem detail to derive PPH dx, not told outright.
11. **Anti-D doses given as IU not "one dose" - 250 IU / 625 IU at specific gestations.** Forces exam-level recall not approximation.
12. **Mild-stem cases with realistic uncertainty.** Q26 - "she remembered being unable to use the speculum during her routine smear two months ago" is a clue that ovary may be ambiguous on exam, plausibly an incidental finding.
13. **Italicised parenthetical guideline citation right inside explanation text.** "(Consensus recommendation 6.48 - Colposcopy referral for atypical glandular/endocervical cells. Cervical screening participants who...)". Treats the reference body as in-line context, not a footnote.
14. **Mechanism explanation gives reason for "compound risk".** Q36 - "the combination with obesity creates a cumulative risk profile that reinforces the contraindication" - teaches the principle of risk compounding rather than memorising single thresholds.
15. **Image-based stems pair clinical photograph with realistic dark-skinned presentation.** Q25 (HFMD), Q28 (syphilis on palms). Avoids the white-skin default.
16. **Vitals include an irrelevant-but-normal abnormal-looking value sometimes (temp 36.5 in OK patient).** Slight desensitisation training; trains "is this in range?" check.

## Transferable principles (any-topic)

These apply equally to A&E, Psychiatry, Medicine, Surgery, GP, Renal, Endo, etc. and should inform the Y4 MCQ Bank:

1. **Template the opening sentence.** "A [age]-year-old [woman/man/child] presents to the [setting] with a [duration] history of [complaint]." Use this every time. Reduce stylistic variance so the reader's cognitive load goes to clinical reasoning.

2. **Vitals in fixed prose order with units, even when normal.** Pulse, BP, RR, SpO2, temperature - always five values. Trains scan-for-abnormal habit. Replace heart-rate units (/min) with the exact convention.

3. **Lab values always with reference range in parens.** No naked numbers. Treat the reader as if seeing the result for the first time.

4. **Discriminator as a measurable detail, never an adjective.** "BP 165/110" not "very high BP". "Mass 8 cm" not "large mass". "Onset 30 minutes ago" not "sudden".

5. **Two demographic-context facts at most, always concrete, sometimes a benign red herring.** Train candidates to use clinical features not stereotypes.

6. **Withhold the dx in the stem.** Options describe actions/diagnoses; the stem describes features. The work is connecting feature -> dx -> action.

7. **5 options, always the same parallel structure.** If one is a procedure, all are procedures. If one is a dx, all are dx. If one is a truth-claim, all are truth-claims.

8. **The correct option's grammatical and dose-format structure must match the four wrong options exactly.** Same units, same modifier pattern.

9. **The lead-in is neutral, "most appropriate", never "best" without qualifier.** "Choose the single best answer" instruction lives below the lead-in.

10. **Explanation opens by re-naming the diagnosis confidently.** "This patient presents with X." No hedging. Reader needs commitment to learn.

11. **Each non-correct option discussed by name in bold paragraph of similar length.** Bold = option text verbatim. Provides equal-weight teaching across the differential.

12. **In-line italic parenthetical citation of the guideline body.** RANZCOG / RACGP / NHMRC / ATAGI / NBA / NIPS / eTG / AMH - named explicitly with the recommendation number where applicable. Anchors to AU practice.

13. **Yellow-block Key Learning Point of 2-3 hyphen-style bullets**: (a) specific decision for this patient, (b) the general rule, (c) the workup/investigation principle.

14. **Mechanism explanation tied to the specific drug/condition, not generic.** E.g. "Mg sulphate acts through multiple mechanisms: calcium antagonist..." beats "Mg sulphate is used in eclampsia".

15. **Use tables in explanations when comparing 3+ similar entities.** Drug vs side effect, syndrome vs USS feature, virus vs gestational risk. Saves prose.

16. **Numerical specificity over qualitative statements.** "5 mg folate", "625 IU anti-D", "0.5-1 cm/hr dilatation", ">=160/110", "BMI >=35 kg/m2", "<5 mm myometrium". Train candidates on actual numbers.

17. **"In context" closing line linking to AU practice / multidisciplinary care / next steps.** Tells the reader what to do AFTER answering correctly.

18. **The presentation feature -> next step axis is the bread and butter.** Most questions test "given X, do Y next" not "what is X?". The bank's Q distribution should weight management questions heavily over pure dx questions.

19. **Withheld threshold detail (e.g. "<5 mm") is the discriminator.** The Q teaches the threshold by forcing the reader to apply it.

20. **Avoid telegraph phrases.** "Sudden severe", "classic for", "highly suggestive of", "characteristic features include" - these signal the answer. Use neutral feature description.

21. **The option list should bracket the same axis.** Time-to-next-test: 6 weeks / 6 months / 1 year. Acuity: observe / GP follow-up / outpatient referral / inpatient / OT. Forces the discriminator to be a specific threshold.

22. **Image when used must add information not in the prose.** HFMD lesions, syphilis palms - both deliver dx-level features (vesicles on dark skin, copper colour) the prose intentionally underspecifies.

23. **Stem length: 110-180 words is the natural exam range.** Shorter = trivia. Longer = padding. Trim ruthlessly to that band unless the case genuinely requires more.

24. **Difficulty is reasoning-work, not topic-obscurity.** Q26 (simple cyst) is graded 0.58 (relatively easy) because the reasoning is short; Q7 (atypical glandular cells, non-16/18 HPV) is 0.15 (very hard) because the algorithm has multiple branches. Match difficulty to reasoning depth, not topic.

25. **Avoid meta-commentary.** Never "this distractor catches people who...". Stems are pure clinical content. Author voice belongs in the explanation alone.
