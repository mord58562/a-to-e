### Q38 (induction-of-labour pulmonary oedema, screenshots 91-92)
- **Stem opening**: "A 30-year-old woman is admitted to the labour ward at 36 weeks gestation with her first pregnancy."
- **Risk stack disclosed gradually**: gestational hypertension diagnosed at 36/40 -> on admission, BP 160/100 mmHg -> urine protein 3+ on dipstick -> "5x boluses of intravenous normal saline over four hours for concerns with fetal CTG" -> "Labour is induced. Fetal CTG normalises and she is started on an oxytocin infusion."
- **Lead-in**: "Which one of the following conditions is this patient at greatest risk of developing?"
- **Investigations panel as bullet list** (prose body, NOT a table): Hb 125 g/L (110-150), platelets 150 x 10^9/L (150-400), ALT 65 U/L (5-40), creatinine 95 micromol/L (45-90). Each value carries its reference range in parentheses on the same line.
- **Distractors**: Chorioamnionitis, Sheehan syndrome, Pulmonary fibrosis, Peripartum cardiomyopathy. Sheehan is a clever foil (also post-partum, also haemodynamic). Pulmonary fibrosis is a pure recognition distractor.
- **Correct answer**: Pulmonary oedema (66% answered correctly).
- **Explanation structure**: One-sentence definition for EACH distractor, then a synthesis paragraph naming the mechanism ("multiple fluid boluses, and oxytocin infusion significantly increases the risk of pulmonary oedema. Oxytocin has an antidiuretic effect, which can exacerbate fluid retention.").
- **Key learning points (orange bullets, 3)**: "Pulmonary oedema is a serious complication of pre-eclampsia, especially when excessive intravenous fluids are administered." / "Careful fluid management is crucial in pre-eclampsia patients, with a preference for controlled infusion over boluses." / "Oxytocin infusion can exacerbate fluid retention and increase the risk of pulmonary oedema in pre-eclampsia patients."
- Time anchor "over four hours" not "240 minutes". Reference ranges inline.

### Q40 (vasa praevia, screenshots 93-94)
- **Stem opening**: "A 32-year-old woman presents to the antenatal clinic for a routine visit at 20 weeks gestation. This is her first pregnancy."

- **Distractor parallelism**: All five are placental/cord conditions, all short noun phrases.
- **Explanation begins with the diagnosis as a one-liner**: "In this scenario, the most concerning condition is vasa praevia."
- **Then sequential exclusion paragraphs** for each distractor, each starting with the bolded condition name.

- **Exam findings as prose**: "There is a 3 cm lump, measuring 3 cm below the level of the left clavicle, with a small, healing scratch is noted on the dorsum of the left hand. The remainder of the examination is unremarkable."
- **Lead-in**: "Which one of the following is the most appropriate management?"
- **Options**: Oral azithromycin, Observation and reassurance (correct), Fine-needle aspiration, Surgical excision of the lymph node, Oral amoxicillin-clavulanate.
- **Counter-intuitive correct answer**: "do nothing".
- **Explanation diagnosis disclosure**: "This patient presents with classic features of cat scratch disease caused by Bartonella henselae."
- **Cites tests by name**: "polymerase chain reaction (PCR) tests" for the organism identification.
- **Orange bullets**: "Cat scratch disease is typically self-limiting, and most immunocompetent patients without systemic symptoms can be managed with observation alone." / "Regional lymphadenopathy is the hallmark of cat scratch disease, typically developing one to three weeks after inoculation with Bartonella henselae."

- **Stem opening**: "A six-year-old girl presents to the Emergency Department with a two-week history of increasing fatigue and pallor. Her parent noticed yellow discolouration of her eyes yesterday. She has no significant medical history and takes no regular medications. Her immunisations are up to date."

## Recurring stem patterns

1. **Opening sentence template**: "A [age]-year-old [woman/girl/man/boy/Aboriginal woman] presents to the [General Practice clinic / Emergency Department / antenatal clinic / hospital] with [presenting complaint or referral context]."
   - Setting often double-tagged: "General Practice clinic" not "GP clinic"; "Emergency Department" not "ED". Spelled-out medical English.
2. **Standardised social negatives** appear in nearly every stem: "He/She has no significant medical history and takes no regular medications. His/her immunisations are up to date." This pattern reassures candidates that nothing has been intentionally withheld in those domains.
3. **"vital signs within normal limits"** as a four-word compression when vitals are not the discriminator (Q42, Q44, Q46, Paeds Q1, Paeds Q3, Paeds Q5, Paeds Q21, Paeds Q22-cat).
4. **Numbered vitals as comma-separated prose** when they ARE the discriminator (Q38, Q40, Q44, Paeds Q20-Turner, Paeds Q22-RPA, Paeds Q2-UTI, Paeds Q26-coarctation, Paeds Q25-haemolysis).
5. **Reference ranges in parentheses inline** with each lab value, only when the value matters for the answer (Q38, Q42, Q44, Paeds Q25).
6. **Bishop score, Bishop score components, BP differential between arm and leg**, etc. are presented WITHOUT naming the diagnostic concept the student must invoke.
7. **Lay-language vomitus descriptors**: "from milky to yellow/green" (Paeds Q1 intussusception) NOT "bilious vomiting".
8. **Exam findings ordered head-to-toe** consistently (general appearance -> vitals -> system-specific findings -> imaging description).
9. **Imaging/photo placement** is always AT THE END of the stem, never embedded mid-paragraph. Captions terse.
10. **Explicit closures of unhelpful avenues**: "A pelvic examination is not performed" (Q44), "There is no recent overseas travel" (cat scratch), "He has no fever or systemic symptoms" (cat scratch), "There was no significant maternal illness or infection" (neonatal UTI).
11. **Age conventions**:
    - Adults: "32-year-old woman"
    - Adolescents: "15-year-old girl" (NOT "teenager")
    - Children: "four-year-old boy" (written out)
    - Infants: "18-month-old boy", "three-day-old boy", "four-month-old boy"
    - Newborns: "A newborn girl is reviewed on the postnatal ward"
12. **Ethnicity/social only disclosed when load-bearing**: Q46 (Aboriginal woman, drives screening rules); Paeds Q21 (mentions "Aboriginal and/or Torres Strait Islander person" only in explanation as ARF risk factor).
13. **Parity & gestation conventions**: "first pregnancy" + "X weeks gestation" together; no "G1P0" notation in stems.
14. **Drug name in social history**: "His medications include baclofen for spasticity" (Paeds Q18). When meds matter, named.
15. **Anchor finding placed late in the stem**, often as the last sentence before the lead-in: "fixed splitting of S2" (ASD), "femoral pulses are weak bilaterally" (CoA), "He is unable to extend his neck to look upwards. He is drooling" (RPA).

## Recurring lead-in patterns

- "Which one of the following is the most likely diagnosis?" (most common)
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?" (second most common)
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate initial antibiotic therapy?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate initial diagnostic investigation?"
- "Which one of the following is the most appropriate treatment?"
- "Which one of the following is of most concern in this situation?" (used when ranking severity, Q40)
- "Which one of the following is the most common extra-intestinal manifestation of this condition in children?" (Q3 coeliac - the only "most common" lead-in)
- "Which one of the following syndromes is most frequently associated with these examination findings?" (Q20 Turner - sideways lead-in that asks about association, not diagnosis)
- "Which one of the following conditions is this patient at greatest risk of developing?" (Q38 - risk-prediction lead-in)
- "Which one of the following is the most likely complication for this patient?" (Paeds Q1 elbow)

No negative lead-ins ("EXCEPT", "NOT") used. No two-part lead-ins. No "follow local guidelines" options.

## Recurring options patterns

1. **Always exactly 5 options**.
2. **Parallel grammatical structure within a Q**: all noun phrases, or all imperative-style management options ("Refer for...", "Arrange...", "Screen...", "Repeat...", "Observation and reassurance").
3. **One "no-action" distractor** appears occasionally and is a viable correct answer ("Observation and reassurance" in cat scratch; "No investigation is required" in intussusception).
4. **Dose-format parity**: when antibiotics are listed, only the drug name is given - no doses (Paeds Q18). This forces selection of agent rather than recall of mg/kg.
5. **Distractor classes**: same anatomical or pathophysiological category as the correct answer (all placental conditions; all syndromes; all antibiotics; all imaging modalities).
6. **Options never name the diagnosis when the stem withholds it** - except where the lead-in is explicitly "most likely diagnosis", in which case the option set IS the differential.
7. **No "all of the above" or "none of the above"**.
8. **Options ordered roughly by clinical likelihood / alphabetical**, not by trickiness; correct answer position varies across positions 1-5.
9. **Per-option vote percentages displayed publicly**: gives the student a calibration cue post-answer.

## Recurring explanation patterns

1. **Sentence 1 names the diagnosis** (when it was withheld): "This patient presents with classic features of cat scratch disease caused by Bartonella henselae." / "The clinical presentation in this case is most consistent with an atrial septal defect (ASD)." / "In this scenario, the most concerning condition is vasa praevia."
2. **Body proceeds option-by-option** with each option's name bolded at the start of its paragraph, and a definition/mechanism + reason it is wrong.
3. **The correct answer paragraph is woven in among the distractors**, not always first.
4. **Mechanism explained briefly** (1-2 sentences) before applying to the case.
5. **A "synthesis" paragraph** often appears that links the components: "Given the presentation of CoA in a newborn girl, Turner syndrome should be strongly suspected and further genetic testing may be warranted."
6. **AU-specific anchors**: guidelines named in body, not in options. ("the 2023 International Evidence-based Guideline for the Assessment and Management of Polycystic Ovary Syndrome"; "The Victorian Royal Children's Hospital guidelines"; "The National Cervical Screening Program guidelines"; "Australian guidelines therefore recommend...").
7. **Source name displayed in body of text** as full institutional name; no URLs, no DOIs, no abbreviations on first mention.
8. **Three orange-bulleted "key learning points"** at the end of nearly every Q. Each bullet is a complete declarative clinical claim with a comparator or caveat.
9. **Hedges used sparingly**: "is typically", "approximately", "is more readily", "is generally reserved for". No "could possibly" or "might be considered".
10. **Length range**: explanations span 1.5-3 visible screens. No 4-line one-paragraph rationales.
11. **Numbers re-cited in explanation** ("WCC 300 x 10^6/L"; "5-15%"; "30%"; "98% sensitivity").
12. **Never says "the correct answer is..."** - the diagnosis is asserted as a clinical fact.
13. **No author meta-commentary** ("this distractor is designed to test..."). Pure clinical content.

## Reusable clinical facts mined

### Obstetrics
- Pulmonary oedema risk in pre-eclampsia driven by: excessive IV fluid + oxytocin's antidiuretic effect (Q38).
- Vasa praevia incidence raised by: low-lying placenta + succenturiate lobe (Q40).
- Vasa praevia management: 20/40 morphology -> repeat colour-Doppler US by maternal-fetal-medicine team -> planned C/S 34-36/40 after antenatal corticosteroids (Q40).
- Bishop score rubric (Q O&G set 2):
  - Dilation: Closed=0, 1-2=1, 3-4=2, 5-6=3
  - Effacement: 0-30=0, 40-50=1, 60-70=2, 80+=3
  - Consistency: Firm=0, Medium=1, Soft=2
  - Position: Posterior=0, Mid=1, Anterior=2
  - Station: -3=0, -2=1, -1/0=2, +1/+2=3

### Gynaecology / adolescent
- Heavy menstrual bleeding in adolescents first-line management = combined OCP (Q44).
- Coagulation factor (vWD) testing only indicated if HMB persists despite COCP or if family history (Q44).
- Transabdominal pelvic US not routinely indicated in adolescent HMB; structural causes rare (Q44).
- Hysteroscopy "invasive, carries risks, and is not indicated as initial management in adolescents with typical presentation of anovulatory bleeding."

### Cervical screening (AU)
- Self-collected hrHPV testing acceptable in all eligible groups including Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people (Q46).
- hrHPV (other) + LBC pLSIL 12 months ago + repeat self-collected hrHPV positive (non-16/18) in an Aboriginal woman -> immediate colposcopy (do not repeat at 12/12) (Q46).
- HPV vaccine school programme covers ages up to 26 in catch-up; not routinely funded after 26 except for specific immunocompromised or MSM contexts (Q46).
- Eligibility window: 25-74 years (Q46).

### Disorders of sex development / delayed puberty
- Complete AIS: 46,XY, female phenotype, breast development (peripheral aromatisation), absent or scant pubic/axillary hair, absent uterus, testosterone in male range with no virilisation (Q42).
- Mullerian agenesis (MRKH): 46,XX, normal female phenotype + normal puberty + absent uterus + normal FSH/LH/E2 (Q42).
- Turner syndrome: short stature, delayed puberty, classic features; mosaicism may mimic delayed puberty without short stature (Q42).
- Constitutional delay: bone age delayed, normal eventual progression (Q42).

### Newborn coarctation / Turner
- BP differential between right arm 100/60 and right leg 40/40 + weak femorals = CoA (Q20).
- 5-15% of CoA patients have Turner syndrome; ~30% of Turner patients have CoA (Q20).
- Noonan: pulmonary valve stenosis, HCM (Q20).
- Williams syndrome: supravalvular AS, renal artery stenosis (Q20).

### Paediatric cardiology
- ASD murmur: mid-systolic ejection, upper LSB, with fixed splitting of S2 (Paeds Q5).
- VSD murmur: pansystolic, lower LSB, smaller VSDs often louder (Paeds Q5).
- HCM: typically associated with fixed splitting of S2 in single-defect ASDs - wait, this Q says HCM produces a systolic ejection murmur, may radiate (Paeds Q5).
- Mitral stenosis: low-pitched mid-diastolic murmur, rare in children (Paeds Q5).
- PDA: continuous machinery murmur (Paeds Q5).
- BP discrepancy + radio-femoral delay = hallmark of CoA (Paeds Q26).
- Echo is initial test for CoA in AU (GP-orderable with Medicare rebate); MRI is gold standard for anatomy but second-line; CTCA reserved for adults; CT chest used post-confirmation; CXR shows rib-notching only in older children (Paeds Q26).

### Paediatric haematology
- Indirect hyperbilirubinaemia + reticulocytosis + splenomegaly = haemolytic anaemia (Paeds Q25).
- DAT is first test (cheapest, fastest) to differentiate immune vs non-immune haemolysis (Paeds Q25).
- Warm AIHA: most common cause of childhood AIHA, often follows recent viral infection (Paeds Q25).
- G6PD deficiency: X-linked recessive, females rarely manifest, requires significant trigger exposure (Paeds Q25).
- Hereditary spherocytosis tested with flow cytometry of red cell membrane proteins (Paeds Q25).

### Paediatric orthopaedics
- Distal humeral supracondylar fracture in children: 50% of elbow fractures (Paeds Q1).
- Most common complication: loss of range of motion (Paeds Q1).
- Brachial artery injury: a recognised but less common complication of displaced supracondylar fractures (Paeds Q1).

### Paediatric infectious disease
- Retropharyngeal abscess (Paeds Q22): age peak 6 months - 4 years; presents with neck stiffness in extension, drooling, fever, irritability, restricted neck extension; lateral cervical spine X-ray or neck CT confirms (Paeds Q22).
- Peritonsillar abscess: trismus, unilateral tonsillar swelling, uvular deviation, mostly older children (Paeds Q22).
- Acute epiglottitis: now rare due to Hib vaccination; rapid onset, drooling, tripod posture, stridor (Paeds Q22).
- Pneumonia in child with cerebral palsy + PEG + aspiration: amox-clav covers anaerobes and beta-lactamase-producers (Paeds Q18).
- Macrolides (azithromycin) reserved for atypical pneumonia (Mycoplasma) (Paeds Q18).
- Cefuroxime reserved for severe pneumonia / non-oral patients (Paeds Q18).
- Ceftriaxone parenteral, reserved for severe disease, masks penicillin susceptibility (Paeds Q18).
- Neonatal UTI: most common SBI in neonates 0-3 months (Paeds Q2 set 2). LP, blood culture, urine culture all required as part of full septic workup. Urine WCC >300 strongly suggests UTI source.
- Cat scratch disease: Bartonella henselae, regional lymphadenopathy 1-3 weeks post inoculation, self-limiting in immunocompetent (Paeds Q22-cat).
- Macrolides only required for immunocompromised, persistent/severe cases of CSD (Paeds Q22-cat).
- Non-bullous impetigo: honey-coloured crusts (Paeds Q21). Topical mupirocin first-line for limited disease in low ARF-risk; oral cefalexin preferred when extensive or high ARF risk (Paeds Q21).

### Paediatric gastroenterology
- Intussusception (Paeds Q1 set 2): age peak 3 months - 3 years; intermittent severe distress, lethargy between episodes, redcurrant jelly stool late, bilious vomiting ("milky to yellow/green"); abdominal ultrasound is the diagnostic test (~98% sensitivity, 98% specificity) (Paeds Q1 set 2).
- Coeliac disease (Paeds Q2 set 2 + Q3):
  - Screen first-degree relatives even if asymptomatic.
  - HLA-DQ2/DQ8 testing: present in 30% of population, but absence has excellent NPV.
  - Anaemia (iron, folate, B12) is the most common extra-intestinal manifestation.
  - Strict gluten-free diet lifelong.
  - DEXA not routinely required initially in paediatric coeliac.
- Vitamin D, B12, folate deficiencies all common (Paeds Q3).

## Surprising or non-obvious craft choices worth copying

1. **Lay-language presenting complaint over jargon**: parents describe "milky to yellow/green" vomit, not "bilious". Forces inference rather than recognition.
2. **Numbers swap places with words for difficulty management**: when vitals are decoys, "vital signs within normal limits"; when load-bearing, full numeric strings.
3. **Reference ranges supplied selectively** - only in labs that the candidate must interpret. A lab with no reference range = a hint it is not the discriminator.
4. **Explicit closures of common red herrings**: "no recent overseas travel", "no fever or systemic symptoms", "A pelvic examination is not performed". This is closing a side door, not telegraphing the front door.
5. **Counter-intuitive correct answer when defensible**: "No investigation is required" / "Observation and reassurance" / "Loss of range of motion" rather than "brachial artery injury". Trains away from pattern-recognition reflexes.
6. **Sideways lead-ins**: "Which syndrome is most frequently associated with these findings?" rather than "What is the diagnosis?" tests a more advanced cognitive task (association mapping).
7. **Single late-stem anchor**: one signature finding placed as the second-to-last sentence ("fixed splitting of S2"; "femoral pulses are weak bilaterally"; "drooling and unable to extend his neck"). Stem is otherwise generic; one phrase decides the question.
8. **Distractors include adjacent diagnoses with one differentiator each**: each option carries exactly one distinguishing feature explained in its rationale (mitral stenosis = mid-diastolic; VSD = pansystolic lower LSB; PDA = continuous machinery). Teaching scaffold built into the wrong-answer rationales.
9. **No options give doses** - even when the right answer is an antibiotic - because dose-recall is not the discriminator the question is set to measure. Dose-discussion lives in the body.
10. **Source named in prose, not in footnote**: "The Victorian Royal Children's Hospital guidelines" appears inside a sentence, increasing recall and avoiding the user having to chase a citation.
11. **Three orange bullets at the end, each independently usable as a flashcard**: declarative, comparator-bearing, mechanism-tagged. Designed as terminal-state summaries.
12. **Cohort-specific demographic only when relevant**: Aboriginal identity in Q46 drives screening rules; in Q22 set 3 (cat scratch) no ethnicity mentioned because it would be irrelevant.
13. **Allowing the same answer text to be both the correct AND the "obvious wrong" depending on stem framing** (cat scratch -> observation correct; suspected lymphoma -> observation would be wrong). The candidate must read the closure-sentences.
14. **Bishop score given as a rubric in the stem** with the requirement that the candidate compute and choose intervention from the result - this is a high-cognitive-work stem because all data is plain and the cognitive load is the application + decision.
15. **Numeric BP differential between arm and leg** as the only abnormality in a routine well-child check - the cognitive work is recognising the discriminator from a single number-pair.

## Transferable principles (any-topic)

1. **One late anchor, generic preamble** - bury exactly one diagnostic clue in the second-to-last sentence; rest of the stem must be plausible for several diagnoses.
2. **Use lay-language patient/parent reporting** for findings the candidate must medicalise (vomit colour, breath pattern, mood shift). Conversely, use medical-English for objective findings (oroscopy, auscultation).
3. **Reference ranges only where the lab is load-bearing**. A lab without a range is a marker that the value is not the answer-key.
4. **Close the red-herring doors explicitly**: "no recent travel", "no fever", "no exposure to X". This is not telegraphing; it is removing avenues the candidate has been trained to consider.
5. **"vital signs within normal limits"** as a compression marker. Save numeric vitals for cases where the numbers matter.
6. **Five options, parallel grammar, same conceptual class**. Mix of action-options when lead-in is management; noun-options when lead-in is diagnosis.
7. **Include one "no action / observation" option** in management Qs - either as a correct answer (cat scratch, ASD) or as a high-tier distractor.
8. **Distractors carry teaching value**: each wrong option has a one-line discriminating feature in its rationale, so the candidate leaves with a 5-way comparator.
9. **Doses absent from options, dose-discussion in explanation body**. Tests selection of agent; doesn't penalise recall failures unrelated to the targeted cognition.
10. **Three orange bullets at the close**: each a complete declarative claim with a comparator, mechanism, or cohort-qualifier. Designed for retention.
11. **Cite the source by full institutional name in the body of the rationale**. "The 2023 International Evidence-based Guideline...", "The National Cervical Screening Program guidelines", "The Australian Immunisation Handbook", "The Victorian Royal Children's Hospital guidelines".
12. **AU specificity comes via guideline name and Medicare/programme reference**, not via "Australian patient" labels.
13. **Demographic descriptors load-bearing only**. Aboriginal identity if the cervical screening pathway is different; Maori/Pacific identity if ARF risk is different; otherwise omitted.
14. **Lead-in variants** for cognitive-task variety: "most likely diagnosis", "most appropriate next step", "most likely complication", "at greatest risk of developing", "most frequently associated with", "of most concern in this situation". Rotating lead-ins prevents pattern-recall.
15. **Sequential history disclosure**: prior result + interval + current result, as in Q46 ("twelve months ago" -> "she has recently"). Allows the candidate to reconstruct a longitudinal pathway.
16. **Explanation paragraph for every option, bolded option text at paragraph head**. Reader can jump to the option they chose.
17. **Counter-intuitive correct answers** when defensible - especially when the obvious choice is over-investigation, over-treatment, or pattern-matching to a higher-acuity differential. Trains the right answer to "the most common complication" or "the most common cause" rather than "the most dangerous".
18. **Numeric anchors as the only abnormality**: a healthy 11-year-old with one BP-differential reading. Cognitive work = recognising what is abnormal in a sea of normal.
19. **Imaging/photo always last in the stem** - never embedded mid-paragraph; caption brief; modality named.
20. **Hedge sparingly**: "is typically", "approximately", "generally reserved for". Forbidden: "may possibly", "could potentially", "might be considered" without a defined trigger.
